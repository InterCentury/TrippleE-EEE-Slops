# 02.03 - Sine Wave

## What is a Sine Wave?

A sine wave is a smooth, periodic oscillation that describes the natural variation of alternating current (AC) voltage and current over time. It is the fundamental waveform for AC power systems because it represents the most efficient and mathematically pure form of alternating energy transfer.

The sine wave is named after the mathematical sine function (sin) from trigonometry. When a rotating vector (phasor) is plotted against time, its vertical projection traces a sine wave. This natural relationship between rotation and oscillation makes sine waves the default output of rotating generators—the source of nearly all electrical power in the world.

## The Mathematics of Sine Waves

The sine wave is derived from the sine function in trigonometry, which describes the ratio of sides in a right triangle.

### The General Sine Wave Equation

The general form of a sine wave includes amplitude, frequency, phase, and offset.

```
GENERAL SINE WAVE EQUATION

    v(t) = A × sin(2πft + φ) + C

    WHERE:
    v(t) = instantaneous voltage at time t (volts)
    A = amplitude (peak voltage, volts)
    f = frequency (hertz, Hz)
    t = time (seconds, s)
    φ = phase angle (radians or degrees)
    C = DC offset (vertical shift, volts)


BREAKDOWN OF EACH PARAMETER:

    1. AMPLITUDE (A):
       Controls the height of the wave
       A = V_peak (maximum voltage from center)
       
       Larger A = taller wave = higher voltage
       
       v(t)
        │
      2A┼    ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲
       A┼  ╱    ╲
        │ ╱      ╲
       0┼╱────────╲──► t
        │
       
       A = 1: amplitude 1 (peak-to-peak 2)
       A = 2: amplitude 2 (peak-to-peak 4)


    2. FREQUENCY (f):
       Controls how fast the wave oscillates
       f = cycles per second (Hz)
       
       Higher f = more cycles per second = shorter period
       
       v(t)
        │
        │   ╱╲╱╲╱╲
        │  ╱  ╲
        │ ╱    ╲
       0┼──────────────► t
       
       f = 1: 1 cycle per second
       f = 2: 2 cycles per second (twice as fast)


    3. PHASE (φ):
       Controls horizontal shift (left or right)
       
       φ positive = shift left (earlier in time)
       φ negative = shift right (later in time)
       
       v(t)
        │       ╱╲
        │      ╱  ╲
        │     ╱    ╲
        │    ╱      ╲
       0┼───╱────────╲──► t
        │  ╱          ╲
        │ ╱            ╲
        │╱              ╲
       
       φ = 0°: no shift (reference)
       φ = 90°: shifted left by 1/4 cycle
       φ = -90°: shifted right by 1/4 cycle


    4. DC OFFSET (C):
       Shifts entire wave up or down
       
       C positive = shift up
       C negative = shift down
       
       v(t)
        │
      2C┼    ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲
       C┼  ╱    ╲
        │ ╱      ╲
       0┼╱────────╲──► t
       
       C = 0: centered on zero (pure AC)
       C = 1: shifted up by 1 (AC + DC)
```

### Phase Angle

Phase angle describes the horizontal shift of a sine wave relative to a reference.

```
PHASE ANGLE (φ)

    GENERAL FORM: v(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft + φ)


    EFFECT OF PHASE SHIFT:

    φ = 0° (reference):
        ╱╲    ╱╲
       ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
      ╱    ╲╱    ╲

    φ = +90° (shift left, leads):
        ╱╲    ╱╲
       ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
      ╱    ╲╱    ╲
      (shifted left by 1/4 cycle)

    φ = -90° (shift right, lags):
           ╱╲    ╱╲
          ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
         ╱    ╲╱    ╲
      (shifted right by 1/4 cycle)

    φ = 180° (inverted):
        ╲╱    ╲╱
         ╲  ╱  ╲  ╱
          ╲╱    ╲╱
      (mirrored vertically)


PHASE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN TWO WAVES:

    v1(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft)
    v2(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft + φ)

    Voltage
        │
    1.0 ┼    ╱╲          ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲        ╱  ╲      v1 (φ = 0°)
        │  ╱    ╲      ╱    ╲
        │ ╱      ╲    ╱      ╲
       0┼╱────────╲──╱────────╲──► Time
        │          ╲╱          ╲
        │           ╲          ╱
        │            ╲        ╱
        │             ╲      ╱      v2 (φ = -45° lagging)
        │              ╲    ╱
        │               ╲  ╱
        │                ╲╱
    -1.0┼

    v2 reaches its peak 45° AFTER v1.
    v2 is said to "lag" v1 by 45°.


PHASE RELATIONSHIP TERMS:

    Term              φ range        Description
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    In phase          φ = 0°         Peaks align exactly
    Phase lead        φ > 0°         Wave peaks earlier
    Phase lag         φ < 0°         Wave peaks later
    Quadrature        φ = ±90°       One peaks when other crosses zero
    Opposite phase    φ = ±180°      Positive peak = negative peak
    Out of phase      φ ≠ 0°         Any non-zero difference


PHASE SHIFT TIME EQUIVALENT:

    Δt = φ / (360° × f)  (for φ in degrees)
    Δt = φ / (2π × f)    (for φ in radians)

    Example: 90° phase shift at 60 Hz
    Δt = 90° / (360° × 60) = 90 / 21600 = 0.00417 s = 4.17 ms

    (This makes sense: 1/4 of 16.67 ms period)
```

## RMS (Root Mean Square) for Sine Waves

RMS is the most important derived parameter for sine wave AC.

```
RMS DERIVATION FOR SINE WAVE

MATHEMATICAL DERIVATION:

    For a sine wave: v(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt)

    Step 1: Square the function
    v²(t) = V_peak² × sin²(ωt)

    Step 2: Take mean over one period
    Average of sin²(ωt) over one cycle = 1/2

    Mean of v²(t) = V_peak² × (1/2) = V_peak² / 2

    Step 3: Take square root
    V_RMS = √(V_peak² / 2) = V_peak / √2

    Therefore: V_RMS = V_peak / √2 ≈ V_peak × 0.7071


WHY RMS IS USEFUL:

    DC:  P = V² / R
    AC:  P_avg = (V_RMS)² / R

    The resistor dissipates the same average power at
    V_RMS AC as at V_DC of the same numeric value!


NUMERICAL VERIFICATION:

    120V DC across 10Ω:
    P = (120)² / 10 = 14400 / 10 = 1440W

    120V RMS AC (170V peak) across 10Ω:
    Instantaneous power varies, but average = 1440W

    Check: At peak (170V): P = (170)² / 10 = 28900 / 10 = 2890W
           At zero (0V):   P = 0W
           Average of sine² wave = (V_peak² / 2) / R
           = (170² / 2) / 10 = (28900 / 2) / 10 = 14450 / 10 = 1445W ✓


RMS VALUES FOR DIFFERENT WAVEFORMS (same V_peak):

    Waveform      V_RMS (relative to V_peak)    Crest Factor (V_peak/V_RMS)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Sine          0.707 × V_peak                1.414
    Square        1.000 × V_peak                1.000
    Triangle      0.577 × V_peak                1.732
    Sawtooth      0.577 × V_peak                1.732
    Half-wave rect 0.500 × V_peak               2.000
```

## Phase Relationships in AC Circuits

### Resistor (In Phase)

In a pure resistor, voltage and current are perfectly aligned.

```
RESISTOR PHASE RELATIONSHIP

    v(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt)
    i(t) = I_peak × sin(ωt)  (no phase shift)

    Voltage and Current:
        ╱╲    ╱╲
       ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
      ╱    ╲╱    ╲

    Phase difference: φ = 0°
    Power factor: PF = 1.0 (perfect)

    Power waveform:
    Power
        │    ╱╲    ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
        │  ╱    ╲╱    ╲
        │ ╱            ╲
       0┼╱──────────────╲──► Time
    
    Power always positive (resistor always consumes)
    Pulses at 2× line frequency (120 Hz for 60 Hz AC)
```

### Capacitor (Current Leads)

In a pure capacitor, current peaks 90° before voltage.

```
CAPACITOR PHASE RELATIONSHIP

    v(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt)
    i(t) = I_peak × sin(ωt + 90°)

    Voltage:     ╱╲    ╱╲
                ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
               ╱    ╲╱    ╲
    Current:     ╱╲    ╱╲
                ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
               ╱    ╲╱    ╲

    Phase difference: φ = +90° (current leads)
    Power factor: PF = 0 (leading)

    Explanation:
    ├── Current flows to charge capacitor plates
    ├── When voltage is zero, current is maximum (charging fastest)
    ├── When voltage is peak, current is zero (fully charged)
    └── Current peaks 1/4 cycle before voltage

    Power waveform:
    Power
        │     ╱╲
        │    ╱  ╲
        │   ╱    ╲
        │  ╱      ╲
       0┼─╱────────╲──► Time
        │╱          ╲
        │            ╲╱
        │             ╲
        │              ╲
      
    Power alternates positive and negative
    Average power = 0 (energy stored and returned)
```

### Inductor (Current Lags)

In a pure inductor, voltage peaks 90° before current.

```
INDUCTOR PHASE RELATIONSHIP

    v(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt)
    i(t) = I_peak × sin(ωt - 90°)

    Voltage:     ╱╲    ╱╲
                ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
               ╱    ╲╱    ╲
    Current:       ╱╲    ╱╲
                  ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
                 ╱    ╲╱    ╲

    Phase difference: φ = -90° (current lags)
    Power factor: PF = 0 (lagging)

    Explanation:
    ├── Inductor opposes change in current
    ├── Voltage must be applied before current can build
    ├── When voltage peaks, current is still rising
    ├── Current peaks 1/4 cycle after voltage
    └── Current lags voltage by 90°

    Power waveform:
    Power
        │        ╱╲
        │       ╱  ╲
        │      ╱    ╲
        │     ╱      ╲
       0┼────╱────────╲──► Time
        │   ╱          ╲
        │  ╱            ╲
        │ ╱              ╲
        │╱                ╲
    
    Power alternates positive and negative
    Average power = 0 (energy stored in magnetic field and returned)
```

## Sine Wave Quality and Distortion

### Total Harmonic Distortion (THD)

Real-world sine waves are not perfect. Harmonics cause distortion.

```
HARMONIC DISTORTION

    Perfect sine wave (fundamental only):
        ╱╲
       ╱  ╲
      ╱    ╲
     ╱      ╲
    ╱        ╲

    Distorted sine wave (with 3rd harmonic):
        ╱╲
       ╱  ╲__
      ╱    ╲
     ╱      ╲__
    ╱        ╲

    The flat top and "fat" shape indicate 3rd harmonic


TOTAL HARMONIC DISTORTION (THD):

    THD = √(V₂² + V₃² + V₄² + ...) / V₁ × 100%

    WHERE:
    V₁ = fundamental (50 Hz or 60 Hz) amplitude
    V₂ = 2nd harmonic amplitude (100 Hz or 120 Hz)
    V₃ = 3rd harmonic amplitude (150 Hz or 180 Hz)
    etc.


TYPICAL THD VALUES:

    Source                    THD        Description
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Grid power (good)         <1%        Very clean
    Grid power (typical)      2-5%       Acceptable
    Pure sine inverter        <3%        Clean
    Modified sine inverter    30-40%     Poor (square-ish)
    Backup generator (good)   5-10%      Moderate
    Backup generator (poor)   15-25%     Dirty
    UPS (standby)             10-20%     Moderate to poor


EFFECTS OF HIGH THD:

    ├── Motors run hotter (harmonic currents)
    ├── Transformers hum more (magnetostriction)
    ├── Capacitors can overheat (high frequency current)
    ├── Sensitive equipment may malfunction
    ├── Power factor correction can fail
    └── Neutral wire overload in three-phase systems
```

### Crest Factor

Crest factor is the ratio of peak to RMS. For pure sine wave, it's √2 ≈ 1.414.

```
CREST FACTOR

    Crest Factor = V_peak / V_RMS


FOR PURE SINE WAVE:

    CF = (V_RMS × √2) / V_RMS = √2 = 1.414


CREST FACTOR FOR DIFFERENT WAVEFORMS:

    Waveform              Crest Factor
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Sine                  1.414
    Square                1.000 (peak = RMS)
    Triangle              1.732
    Sawtooth              1.732
    Pulse (10% duty)      3.162
    Pulse (1% duty)       10.000


WHY CREST FACTOR MATTERS:

    Measurement equipment:
    ├── Average-responding meters assume CF = 1.414
    ├── For non-sine waves, they read incorrectly
    └── True RMS meters measure correctly for any CF

    Amplifier design:
    ├── Audio peaks have high crest factor (music)
    ├── Amplifier must handle peaks without clipping
    └── Power supply must provide peak current

    Example: 100W RMS audio with CF = 3
    Peak power = 100 × 3² = 900W!
    Amplifier must handle 900W peaks for clean sound
```

## Sine Wave in Three-Phase Systems

Three-phase power uses three sine waves 120° apart.

```
THREE-PHASE SINE WAVES

    Voltage
        │    ╱╲         ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲   ╱╲  ╱  ╲
        │  ╱    ╲ ╱  ╲╱    ╲
        │ ╱      ╱    ╲     ╲
       0┼╱──────╱──────╲─────╲──► Time
        │       ╲      ╱     ╱
        │        ╲    ╱     ╱
        │         ╲  ╱     ╱
        │          ╲╱     ╱
        │                ╱
        │               ╱


    EQUATIONS:

    Phase A: v_A(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt)
    Phase B: v_B(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt - 120°)
    Phase C: v_C(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt - 240°) = V_peak × sin(ωt + 120°)


    KEY PROPERTIES:

    ├── Phases are 120° apart (1/3 cycle)
    ├── Sum of all three voltages = 0 at any instant
    ├── v_A + v_B + v_C = 0
    ├── Line-to-line voltage = √3 × line-to-neutral
    ├── Constant total power (no pulsations)
    └── Rotating magnetic field in motors


    THREE-PHASE POWER:

    P_total = 3 × V_phase × I_phase × PF
           = √3 × V_line × I_line × PF

    Example: 208V line-to-line, 10A, PF = 0.8
    P = √3 × 208 × 10 × 0.8 = 1.732 × 208 × 10 × 0.8
    P = 1.732 × 1664 = 2880W (approx)

    Same wires as single-phase 208V, 10A:
    Single-phase power = 208 × 10 × 0.8 = 1664W
    Three-phase delivers 73% more power with same wires!
```

| Instantaneous | v(t) | V_peak × sin(ωt) | Varies with time |

| Peak-to-peak | V_pp | V_p × 2 | 2 × V_p |
| RMS | V_RMS | V_p / √2 | 0.7071 × V_p |

## Summary

1. **Sine wave** is the natural waveform for AC power, described by v(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft + φ)

2. **Key parameters:** Amplitude (peak), frequency (cycles/second), period (time/cycle), phase (horizontal shift)

3. **Peak voltage** (V_p) is maximum instantaneous value; V_pp = V_p × 2

4. **RMS (Root Mean Square)** = V_p / √2 ≈ 0.7071 × V_p; equates AC to DC for heating

5. **Frequency** f = 1/T; power grids use 50 Hz (EU) or 60 Hz (US)

6. **Phase** describes timing between waves; φ = 0° (in phase), ±90° (quadrature), 180° (opposite)

7. **Rotating generators** naturally produce sine waves: v(t) = B × A × N × ω × sin(ωt)

8. **Electronic oscillators** generate sine waves: Wien bridge (audio), LC (RF), crystal (precise)

9. **Resistor:** voltage and current in phase (φ = 0°, PF = 1)

10. **Capacitor:** current leads voltage by 90° (φ = +90°, PF = 0 leading)

11. **Inductor:** current lags voltage by 90° (φ = -90°, PF = 0 lagging)

12. **Three-phase system:** three sine waves 120° apart; constant power, rotating magnetic field

13. **THD (Total Harmonic Distortion)** measures sine wave purity; grid <5%, modified inverter >30%

14. **Crest factor** = V_p / V_RMS; sine wave = 1.414; important for amplifier and meter design
