# 01.07 - Electrical Power (The Watt)

## What is Electrical Power?

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred or converted. It measures how quickly work is done or energy is used by an electrical device. When you turn on a light bulb, power determines how bright it shines. When you run a motor, power determines how much mechanical work it can do.

The unit of power is the **Watt** (symbol: W), named after Scottish inventor **James Watt** (1736-1819), who developed the steam engine. One watt is equal to one joule of energy per second.

## The Basic Power Formula

The fundamental relationship between power, voltage, and current is simple and elegant.

```
BASIC POWER FORMULA

    P = V × I

WHERE:
P = Power in Watts (W)
V = Voltage in Volts (V)
I = Current in Amperes (A)


DEFINITION OF ONE WATT:

    1 Watt = 1 Volt × 1 Ampere
    
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                             │
    │   1V ──► ┌─────────────┐ ──► 1A             │
    │          │    LOAD     │                    │
    │          │    1Ω       │                    │
    │          └─────────────┘                    │
    │                                             │
    │   Power = 1V × 1A = 1 WATT                  │
    │                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘


ENERGY PER SECOND:

    1 Watt = 1 Joule per second
    
    If a 100W light bulb is on for 1 second:
    100 Joules of electrical energy become:
    ├── Light energy (about 10 Joules)
    └── Heat energy (about 90 Joules)
```

## Power Formulas Derived from Ohm's Law

Since V = I × R, we can substitute into P = V × I to get two additional formulas.

```
THE THREE POWER FORMULAS

FORMULA 1 (Basic):
    P = V × I
    Use when you know Voltage AND Current


FORMULA 2 (Using Current and Resistance):
    Substitute V = I × R into P = V × I
    P = (I × R) × I
    P = I² × R
    
    Use when you know Current AND Resistance


FORMULA 3 (Using Voltage and Resistance):
    Substitute I = V / R into P = V × I
    P = V × (V / R)
    P = V² / R
    
    Use when you know Voltage AND Resistance


POWER WHEEL (Complete set):

                    ┌─────────────┐
                    │      P      │
                    │   (Watts)   │
                    └─────────────┘
                          │
            ┌─────────────┼─────────────┐
            │             │             │
       ┌────┴────┐   ┌────┴────┐   ┌────┴────┐
       │    V    │   │    I    │   │    R    │
       │ (Volts) │   │ (Amps)  │   │ (Ohms)  │
       └────┬────┘   └────┬────┘   └────┬────┘
            │             │             │
            └─────────────┼─────────────┘
                          │
        ┌─────────────────┼─────────────────┐
        │                 │                 │
        ▼                 ▼                 ▼
    P = V × I         P = I² × R        P = V² / R
    V = P / I         I = √(P/R)        V = √(P×R)
    I = P / V         R = P / I²        R = V² / P
```

## Practical Power Calculations

### Example 1: Finding Power from Voltage and Current

```
PROBLEM:
A laptop charger outputs 19V at 3.42A. How much power does it deliver?

GIVEN:
V = 19V
I = 3.42A
P = ?

FORMULA:
P = V × I

CALCULATION:
P = 19V × 3.42A = 65W

ANSWER:
The charger delivers 65 watts.


VERIFICATION:
Typical laptop power: 45W-90W
65W is reasonable for a mid-sized laptop
```

### Example 2: Finding Power from Current and Resistance

```
PROBLEM:
A 0.5A current flows through a 100Ω resistor. How much power does the resistor dissipate?

GIVEN:
I = 0.5A
R = 100Ω
P = ?

FORMULA:
P = I² × R

CALCULATION:
P = (0.5)² × 100 = 0.25 × 100 = 25W

ANSWER:
The resistor dissipates 25 watts (as heat).


REALITY CHECK:
A typical small resistor is 1/4W (0.25W)
25W would instantly burn a small resistor!
You would need a large 25W wirewound resistor.
```

### Example 3: Finding Power from Voltage and Resistance

```
PROBLEM:
A 240Ω resistor is connected across 12V. What power does it consume?

GIVEN:
V = 12V
R = 240Ω
P = ?

FORMULA:
P = V² / R

CALCULATION:
P = (12)² / 240 = 144 / 240 = 0.6W

ANSWER:
The resistor dissipates 0.6 watts.


SELECTING RESISTOR:
0.6W requires at least 1W resistor (safety margin)
A 1/2W (0.5W) resistor would overheat
Use 1W or higher power rating
```

### Example 4: Finding Current from Power and Voltage

```
PROBLEM:
A 1500W space heater runs on 120V. How much current does it draw?

GIVEN:
P = 1500W
V = 120V
I = ?

FORMULA:
I = P / V

CALCULATION:
I = 1500W / 120V = 12.5A

ANSWER:
The heater draws 12.5 amperes.


SAFETY CHECK:
Household circuit breakers:
├── 15A breaker → OK (12.5A < 15A)
├── But don't run other high-power devices on same circuit
└── Dedicated circuit recommended for space heaters
```

### Example 5: Finding Resistance from Power and Voltage

```
PROBLEM:
A 60W incandescent light bulb is rated for 120V. What is its resistance?

GIVEN:
P = 60W
V = 120V
R = ?

FORMULA:
R = V² / P

CALCULATION:
R = (120)² / 60 = 14400 / 60 = 240Ω

ANSWER:
The hot filament resistance is 240 ohms.


IMPORTANT NOTE:
Cold resistance is much lower (about 15Ω)
This is why bulbs have high inrush current
When cold: I = 120V / 15Ω = 8A (instantaneous)
When hot: I = 120V / 240Ω = 0.5A (steady state)
```

### Example 6: Finding Voltage from Power and Resistance

```
PROBLEM:
A 100W resistor (designed to dissipate 100W) has 4Ω resistance.
What voltage should be applied?

GIVEN:
P = 100W
R = 4Ω
V = ?

FORMULA:
V = √(P × R)

CALCULATION:
V = √(100 × 4) = √400 = 20V

ANSWER:
Apply 20V to get 100W power dissipation.


VERIFICATION:
I = V / R = 20V / 4Ω = 5A
P = V × I = 20V × 5A = 100W ✓
```

## Power in Series Circuits

In a series circuit, power distributes based on resistance. Higher resistance = more power.

```
SERIES CIRCUIT POWER EXAMPLE

    ┌─────[R1]─────[R2]─────[R3]───────┐
    │      10Ω       20Ω       30Ω     │
    │                                  │
    └─────────────[12V]────────────────┘

STEP 1: Find total resistance
R_total = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60Ω

STEP 2: Find current
I = V / R_total = 12V / 60Ω = 0.2A

STEP 3: Find power for each resistor
P₁ = I² × R₁ = (0.2)² × 10 = 0.04 × 10 = 0.4W
P₂ = I² × R₂ = (0.2)² × 20 = 0.04 × 20 = 0.8W
P₃ = I² × R₃ = (0.2)² × 30 = 0.04 × 30 = 1.2W

STEP 4: Find total power
P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ = 0.4 + 0.8 + 1.2 = 2.4W

VERIFICATION using P = V × I:
P_total = 12V × 0.2A = 2.4W ✓

OBSERVATIONS:
├── Largest resistor (30Ω) dissipates MOST power (1.2W)
├── Smallest resistor (10Ω) dissipates LEAST power (0.4W)
└── In series, power follows resistance (P ∝ R)
```

## Power in Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, power distributes based on conductance (1/R). Lower resistance = more power.

```
PARALLEL CIRCUIT POWER EXAMPLE

              ┌─────[R1]─────┐
              │     10Ω      │
              │              │
              ├─────[R2]─────┤
              │     20Ω      │
              │              │
              ├─────[R3]─────┤
              │     30Ω      │
              │              │
            (+)            (-)
              │              │
              └─────[12V]────┘

STEP 1: Find voltage across each resistor
V₁ = V₂ = V₃ = 12V (parallel)

STEP 2: Find current through each resistor
I₁ = V / R₁ = 12V / 10Ω = 1.2A
I₂ = V / R₂ = 12V / 20Ω = 0.6A
I₃ = V / R₃ = 12V / 30Ω = 0.4A

STEP 3: Find power for each resistor
P₁ = V × I₁ = 12V × 1.2A = 14.4W
P₂ = V × I₂ = 12V × 0.6A = 7.2W
P₃ = V × I₃ = 12V × 0.4A = 4.8W

STEP 4: Find total power
P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ = 14.4 + 7.2 + 4.8 = 26.4W

VERIFICATION using P = V² / R_eq:
1/R_eq = 1/10 + 1/20 + 1/30 = 0.1 + 0.05 + 0.0333 = 0.1833
R_eq = 5.45Ω
P_total = (12)² / 5.45 = 144 / 5.45 = 26.4W ✓

OBSERVATIONS:
├── Smallest resistor (10Ω) dissipates MOST power (14.4W)
├── Largest resistor (30Ω) dissipates LEAST power (4.8W)
└── In parallel, power follows conductance (P ∝ 1/R)
```

## AC Power: Real, Reactive, and Apparent

In AC circuits, power is more complex due to phase shifts between voltage and current.

```
AC POWER TYPES

REAL POWER (P) - Watts (W)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    Actual power that does useful work
    Heats resistors, lights bulbs, turns motors
    Formula: P = V × I × cos(φ)
    Where cos(φ) = power factor


REACTIVE POWER (Q) - Volt-Amperes Reactive (VAR)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    Power stored and returned by inductors and capacitors
    Does no useful work but loads the grid
    Formula: Q = V × I × sin(φ)


APPARENT POWER (S) - Volt-Amperes (VA)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    Vector sum of real and reactive power
    What utilities must supply
    Formula: S = V × I


POWER TRIANGLE:

                  S (Apparent)
                 /|
                / |
               /  | Q (Reactive)
              /   |
             /    |
            / φ   |
           /______|
           P (Real)
    
    Relationship: S² = P² + Q²
    Power Factor = P / S = cos(φ)


EXAMPLES:

Resistive load (heater, incandescent bulb):
├── φ = 0°, cos(φ) = 1
├── P = V × I, Q = 0
└── Power factor = 1 (perfect)

Inductive load (motor, transformer):
├── φ = 30°, cos(φ) = 0.866
├── P = V × I × 0.866
├── Q = V × I × 0.5
└── Power factor = 0.866 (lagging)

Capacitive load (power supply input):
├── φ = -30°, cos(φ) = 0.866
├── P = V × I × 0.866
├── Q = V × I × -0.5
└── Power factor = 0.866 (leading)
```

## Power Efficiency

No device is 100% efficient. Input power always exceeds output power.

```
EFFICIENCY FORMULA

    Output Power
    η = ──────────── × 100%
    Input Power

WHERE:
η = Efficiency (percent)
Output Power = Useful power delivered (W)
Input Power = Total power consumed (W)


EFFICIENCY EXAMPLES:

Device                    Efficiency    Lost as Heat
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Incandescent bulb         2-5%          95-98%
LED bulb                  20-40%        60-80%
Fluorescent bulb          15-25%        75-85%
Small electric motor      50-70%        30-50%
Large industrial motor    85-95%        5-15%
Power supply (linear)     40-60%        40-60%
Power supply (switching)  70-90%        10-30%
Solar panel               15-22%        78-85%
Car engine                20-30%        70-80%
Electric vehicle motor    85-92%        8-15%
Transformer               95-98%        2-5%
Human body                20-25%        75-80%


CALCULATION EXAMPLE:

PROBLEM:
A power supply draws 50W from the wall.
It delivers 40W to the computer.
What is its efficiency?

SOLUTION:
η = (40W / 50W) × 100% = 80%

LOST POWER:
P_lost = 50W - 40W = 10W (dissipated as heat)


IMPLICATIONS:
├── 80% efficient means 20% wasted as heat
├── 50W input → 40W useful + 10W heat
├── Must remove 10W of heat (ventilation, heatsinks)
└── Higher efficiency = less heat = smaller cooling
```

## Energy: Power Over Time

Power is instantaneous. Energy is power integrated over time.

```
ENERGY FORMULA

    E = P × t

WHERE:
E = Energy in Joules (J) or Watt-seconds
P = Power in Watts (W)
t = Time in seconds (s)


PRACTICAL UNITS:

    Joule (J)      = 1 Watt × 1 second (basic unit)
    Watt-hour (Wh) = 1 Watt × 1 hour (utility billing)
    Kilowatt-hour (kWh) = 1000 Wh (what you pay for)


CONVERSIONS:

    1 kWh = 1000 Wh = 3,600,000 Joules


COST CALCULATION:

    Cost = Energy (kWh) × Rate ($ per kWh)


EXAMPLES:

Example 1: 60W bulb for 5 hours
    Energy = 60W × 5h = 300 Wh = 0.3 kWh
    Cost at $0.12/kWh = 0.3 × $0.12 = $0.036 (3.6 cents)

Example 2: 1500W heater for 8 hours
    Energy = 1500W × 8h = 12000 Wh = 12 kWh
    Cost at $0.12/kWh = 12 × $0.12 = $1.44

Example 3: 10W LED bulb for 30 days (8 hours/day)
    Hours = 30 × 8 = 240 hours
    Energy = 10W × 240h = 2400 Wh = 2.4 kWh
    Cost at $0.12/kWh = 2.4 × $0.12 = $0.288 (29 cents)

Example 4: Compare 60W incandescent vs 10W LED
    Both produce same light (800 lumens)
    1000 hours of use:
    
    Incandescent: 60W × 1000h = 60 kWh → $7.20
    LED:          10W × 1000h = 10 kWh → $1.20
    
    SAVINGS: $6.00 per 1000 hours
```

## Power and Heat (Joule Heating)

When current flows through resistance, heat is generated. This is called Joule heating or I²R loss.

```
JOULE HEATING PRINCIPLE

    Heat generated per second = I² × R

    More current = MUCH more heat (I² relationship)
    Higher resistance = more heat (for same current)


HEATING EXAMPLES:

Current    Resistance    Heat (I²R)    Result
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1A         1Ω            1W            Warm
2A         1Ω            4W            Hot
3A         1Ω            9W            Very hot
4A         1Ω            16W           Glowing red
5A         1Ω            25W           Glowing orange

1A         10Ω           10W           Very hot
1A         100Ω          100W          Glowing red
1A         1000Ω         1000W         White hot


PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:

Wire sizing:
├── Too much current through thin wire = heat = fire
├── Breakers and fuses protect against overcurrent
└── Larger wire = lower resistance = less heat

Component ratings:
├── Resistors have power ratings (1/4W, 1/2W, 1W, etc.)
├── Exceeding rating = overheating = failure
└── Heat sinks needed for power transistors

Efficiency:
├── I²R losses are wasted energy
├── Higher voltage = lower current = lower I²R losses
└── Power lines use high voltage (hundreds of kV)
```

## Common Power Problems and Solutions

### Problem 1: Power Supply Overload

```
SYMPTOM:
├── Power supply shuts down or voltage drops
├── Supply gets very hot
├── Voltage sags under load

DIAGNOSIS:
Calculate total power of all loads
Compare to power supply rating

EXAMPLE:
Power supply: 12V, 5A (60W maximum)
Connected loads:
├── Motor: 12V, 3A → 36W
├── LEDs: 12V, 1A → 12W
├── Controller: 12V, 0.5A → 6W
└── TOTAL: 54W (close to 60W limit)

Add another 1A load (12W):
New total: 66W (EXCEEDS 60W rating)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Remove some loads
├── Use higher wattage power supply
├── Distribute loads across multiple supplies
└── Use more efficient components
```

### Problem 2: Component Overheating

```
SYMPTOM:
├── Component too hot to touch
├── Discoloration or burning smell
├── Premature failure

DIAGNOSIS:
Calculate actual power dissipation
Compare to component rating

EXAMPLE:
Resistor: 100Ω, rated 1/4W (0.25W)
Applied voltage: 12V

Actual power: P = V² / R = 144 / 100 = 1.44W
1.44W >> 0.25W (nearly 6× over rating)

RESULT: Resistor will burn instantly!

SOLUTIONS:
├── Use higher wattage resistor (2W or 5W)
├── Increase resistance (if circuit allows)
├── Reduce voltage
├── Use multiple resistors in series/parallel

For 12V across 100Ω:
Need resistor rated >1.44W → use 2W or 5W
Or use four 400Ω resistors in parallel (each dissipates 0.36W)
```

### Problem 3: High Energy Costs

```
SYMPTOM:
High electricity bills

DIAGNOSIS:
Identify largest power consumers
Calculate operating hours

COMMON CULPRITS:
├── Space heaters (1500W)
├── Air conditioners (2000-5000W)
├── Water heaters (3000-4500W)
├── Clothes dryers (2000-4000W)
├── Old refrigerators (200-400W)
├── Incandescent bulbs (60-100W each)
└── Phantom loads (standby power, 1-10W each)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace incandescent with LED (1/6 the power)
├── Unplug devices when not in use (kill phantom loads)
├── Use power strips with switches
├── Upgrade old appliances (more efficient)
├── Insulate better (reduce heating/cooling needs)
├── Use timers and smart plugs
└── Consider solar panels for long-term savings

CALCULATION EXAMPLE:
Replace 10 incandescent bulbs (60W each, 5 hours/day)
with 10 LED bulbs (10W each, 5 hours/day)

Daily savings = (10×60W - 10×10W) × 5h = 500W × 5h = 2500 Wh = 2.5 kWh
Yearly savings = 2.5 × 365 = 912.5 kWh
At $0.12/kWh: $109.50 per year
```

## Summary

1. **Power** is the rate of doing work or using energy (P = V × I)

2. **Unit:** Watt (W) = 1 Volt × 1 Ampere = 1 Joule per second

3. **Three power formulas:**
   - P = V × I (basic)
   - P = I² × R (use with current and resistance)
   - P = V² / R (use with voltage and resistance)

4. **Derived formulas:**
   - I = P / V
   - V = P / I
   - R = V² / P
   - I = √(P/R)
   - V = √(P×R)

5. **Series circuits:** Largest resistor dissipates most power (P ∝ R)

6. **Parallel circuits:** Smallest resistor dissipates most power (P ∝ 1/R)

7. **AC power types:**
   - Real (P) - does work
   - Reactive (Q) - stored/returned
   - Apparent (S) - total supplied

8. **Power factor = P/S** (1 is perfect, lower is inefficient)

9. **Energy = Power × Time** (kWh is what you pay for)

10. **Efficiency = P_out / P_in** (always less than 100%)

11. **Joule heating = I²R** (current dominates heat generation)

12. **Higher voltage = lower current = lower losses** (for same power)
