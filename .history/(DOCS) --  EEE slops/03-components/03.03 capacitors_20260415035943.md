# 03.03 - Capacitors

## What is a Capacitor?

A capacitor is a passive two-terminal electronic component that stores electrical energy in an electric field. It consists of two conductive plates separated by an insulating material called a dielectric. When voltage is applied, opposite charges accumulate on the two plates, storing energy.

The capacitor was invented in 1745 by Ewald Georg von Kleist and independently by Pieter van Musschenbroek (the Leyden jar). Today, capacitors are essential components in virtually all electronic circuits, used for filtering, energy storage, timing, coupling, decoupling, and many other applications.

### The Unit of Capacitance: The Farad

```
THE FARAD (F)

DEFINITION:
1 Farad = 1 Coulomb of charge per 1 Volt

1 F = 1 C / 1 V


VISUALIZING 1 FARAD:

    A 1F capacitor with 1V across it stores 1 Coulomb
    1 Coulomb = 6.24 × 10¹⁸ electrons!
    
    1 Farad is HUGE. Most capacitors are much smaller.


COMMON CAPACITANCE PREFIXES:

    Prefix      Symbol      Value           Example
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Farad       F           1 F             Supercapacitor
    Millifarad  mF          0.001 F         1000 µF
    Microfarad  µF          0.000001 F      10 µF (common)
    Nanofarad   nF          0.000000001 F   100 nF (very common)
    Picofarad   pF          0.000000000001 F 22 pF (small)


TYPICAL CAPACITANCE VALUES:

    Value       Use
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    1-100 pF    Tuning circuits, RF, oscillators
    100 pF-1 nF High-frequency decoupling
    1-100 nF    General decoupling, filtering
    100 nF      Most common decoupling capacitor
    1-100 µF    Power supply filtering, audio coupling
    100-1000 µF Large power supply filters
    1-100 F     Supercapacitors (energy storage, backup)
```

### Supercapacitors

Very high capacitance (1F to 5000F+) for energy storage.

```

    │   │                                                     │   │

    │   │   │  Activated carbon electrode (+)           │    │   │
    │   │   │  Electrolyte                             │    │   │
    │   │   │  Separator                               │    │   │

    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Characteristics:
    ├── Extremely high capacitance (1F to 5000F+)
    ├── Low voltage (2.5V to 2.7V typical per cell)
    ├── Low ESR (compared to batteries)
    ├── Very high cycle life (500,000+ cycles)
    ├── Can charge/discharge very quickly
    ├── Lower energy density than batteries
    ├── Use: Backup power, burst power, regenerative braking
```

### Voltage Rating

The maximum DC voltage that can be applied without damage.

```
VOLTAGE RATING IMPORTANCE

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  CRITICAL: Never exceed voltage rating!                    │
    │                                                             │
    │  Exceeding voltage rating may cause:                        │
    │  ├── Dielectric breakdown (punctured)                      │
    │  ├── Short circuit (capacitor becomes wire)                │
    │  ├── Explosion (electrolytic, tantalum)                    │
    │  ├── Fire (especially tantalum)                            │
    │  └── Permanent damage                                      │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Rule: Use capacitor rated for at least 1.5× operating voltage
    Example: 12V circuit → use 16V or 25V capacitor


TYPICAL VOLTAGE RATINGS:

    Value       Common Uses
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    6.3V        Low-voltage digital (1.8V, 3.3V)
    10V         USB power (5V)
    16V         Common for 12V circuits (1.5× margin)
    25V         12V systems (2× margin)
    35V         24V systems (1.5× margin)
    50V         General purpose, 12/24V with margin
    63V         Audio coupling, higher voltage
    100V        Tube circuits, some power supplies
    250V+       AC line filtering, snubbers
    400V-450V   Power supply input (after rectifier)
    1000V+      High-voltage, CRT, Tesla coils


DERATING FOR RELIABILITY:

    Application                 Derating Factor
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    Commercial                 80% (use 25V for 20V)
    Industrial                 70%
    Military/aerospace         50%
    Tantalum capacitors        50% (MANDATORY!)
```

### Temperature Rating

Maximum operating temperature affects lifetime.

```
TEMPERATURE RATINGS

    Rating        Max Temp     Typical Application
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    85°C          185°F        Consumer, indoor
    105°C         221°F        Industrial, power supplies
    125°C         257°F        Automotive, engine bay
    150°C+        302°F+       Military, aerospace


LIFETIME vs TEMPERATURE (Electrolytic capacitors):

    Lifetime doubles for every 10°C below rating!
    
    Example: 2000 hour capacitor at 105°C rating
    ├── At 105°C: 2000 hours (83 days)
    ├── At 95°C:  4000 hours (167 days)
    ├── At 85°C:  8000 hours (333 days)
    ├── At 65°C:  32,000 hours (3.7 years)
    └── At 45°C:  128,000 hours (14.6 years)
    
    Rule: Keep capacitors cool for long life!
```

### Equivalent Series Resistance (ESR)

ESR is the internal resistance of a capacitor, important for high-current applications.

```
ESR EXPLANATION

    Capacitor model:
    
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │   Ideal capacitor ───┬─── ESR (resistance) ───┬───         │
    │                      │                         │           │
    │                      │    ┌─────────┐          │           │
    │                      └────┤ ESL     ├──────────┘           │
    │                           │(inductance)                    │
    │                           └─────────┘                      │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    ESR causes:
    ├── Power dissipation (heat) = I_ripple² × ESR
    ├── Voltage drop under ripple current
    ├── Reduced filtering effectiveness
    └── Premature failure if too high


TYPICAL ESR VALUES (at 100 kHz):

    Capacitor Type              ESR (Ω)         Use
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Electrolytic (general)      0.5 - 5.0       Power filtering
    Electrolytic (low ESR)      0.05 - 0.5      SMPS output
    Electrolytic (ultra-low ESR) 0.01 - 0.1     CPU decoupling
    Tantalum                    0.5 - 3.0       General
    Tantalum (low ESR)          0.1 - 0.5       Decoupling
    Ceramic (X7R, X5R)          0.01 - 0.1      Decoupling
    Ceramic (C0G/NP0)           0.001 - 0.01    RF, precision
    Film                        0.01 - 0.5      Audio, snubbers
    Supercapacitor              0.01 - 1.0      Energy storage


WHEN ESR MATTERS:

    ├── Switching power supplies (output filtering)
    ├── CPU/GPU decoupling (high ripple current)
    ├── Audio coupling (low distortion)
    ├── Motor drives (high current)
    └── Battery smoothing (large ripple)
```

## Capacitors in Circuits

### Charging and Discharging (RC Time Constant)

The time constant determines how quickly a capacitor charges or discharges.

```
RC TIME CONSTANT

    τ = R × C

    WHERE:
    τ = time constant (seconds)
    R = resistance (ohms)
    C = capacitance (farads)


CHARGING (through resistor R):

    Voltage across capacitor:
    V_c(t) = V_final × (1 - e^(-t/τ))
    
    Time to reach certain percentage:
    ├── 1τ (63.2%)     (t = R×C)
    ├── 2τ (86.5%)
    ├── 3τ (95.0%)
    ├── 4τ (98.2%)
    └── 5τ (99.3%)     (considered fully charged)


DISCHARGING (through resistor R):

    V_c(t) = V_initial × e^(-t/τ)
    
    Time to discharge:
    ├── 1τ: 36.8% remaining
    ├── 2τ: 13.5% remaining
    ├── 3τ: 5.0% remaining
    ├── 4τ: 1.8% remaining
    └── 5τ: 0.7% remaining (considered discharged)


GRAPHICAL VIEW:

    Voltage (%)
        │
    100 ┼────────────────────────────────────────
        │                                      
     90 ┼                                      
        │                                      
     80 ┼                                      
        │                                      
     70 ┼                                      
        │                                      
     63 ┼─────────●                             (1τ)
        │         ╲                             
     50 ┼          ╲                            
        │           ╲                           
     37 ┼            ●                          (1τ discharge)
        │             ╲                         
     30 ┼              ╲                        
        │               ╲                       
     20 ┼                ╲                      
        │                 ╲                     
     10 ┼                  ╲                    
        │                   ╲                   
      5 ┼                    ╲                  
        │                     ╲                 
      0 ┼──────────────────────●───────────────► Time
        0    1τ    2τ    3τ    4τ    5τ


PRACTICAL EXAMPLES:

    Example 1: R = 10kΩ, C = 100µF
    τ = 10,000 × 0.0001 = 1 second
    
    After 1 second: capacitor charged to 63% of supply
    After 5 seconds: 99% charged (essentially full)

    Example 2: R = 1kΩ, C = 1µF
    τ = 1000 × 0.000001 = 0.001 second = 1ms
    
    Fast charging! Used for timing in oscillators.
```

### Capacitors in Series

```
CAPACITORS IN SERIES

    1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ... + 1/C_n

    For two capacitors: C_total = (C₁ × C₂) / (C₁ + C₂)


EXAMPLE:

    ┌───[10µF]───[22µF]───[47µF]───┐
    │                               │
    │                               │
    └─────────────[V]───────────────┘

    1/C_total = 1/10 + 1/22 + 1/47
               = 0.1 + 0.04545 + 0.02128
               = 0.16673
    C_total = 1 / 0.16673 = 6.0µF


CHARACTERISTICS:
├── Total capacitance < smallest individual capacitor
├── Charge same on all capacitors (Q = C×V same)
├── Voltage divides across capacitors (inversely proportional to C)
├── Adding capacitors in series DECREASES total capacitance
├── Use: Higher voltage rating (voltage divides)
└── Use: Smaller capacitance than available


VOLTAGE DIVISION FOR CAPACITORS (DC):

    V_n = V_total × (C_total / C_n)
    
    Example: 10µF and 22µF in series, 12V total
    C_total = 6.9µF
    V₁ = 12 × (6.9/10) = 8.3V
    V₂ = 12 × (6.9/22) = 3.7V
    
    Note: Different from resistors!
```

### Capacitors in Parallel

```
CAPACITORS IN PARALLEL

    C_total = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ... + C_n


EXAMPLE:

              ┌─────[10µF]─────┐
              │                │
              ├─────[22µF]─────┤
              │                │
              ├─────[47µF]─────┤
              │                │
            (+)               (-)
              │                │
              └─────[V]────────┘

    C_total = 10 + 22 + 47 = 79µF


CHARACTERISTICS:
├── Total capacitance = sum of all
├── Voltage same across all capacitors
├── Charge adds: Q_total = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃
├── Adding capacitors in parallel INCREASES total capacitance
├── Use: Larger capacitance than available
├── Use: Lower ESR (parallel reduces ESR)
└── Use: Redundancy (if one fails open, others work)
```

## Capacitor Applications

### Decoupling (Bypass) Capacitors

The most common use: removing noise from power supplies.

```
DECOUPLING CAPACITOR PLACEMENT

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │   Power supply ──┬──────────────────────────┬── IC Vcc     │
    │                  │                          │              │
    │                  │    ┌───────┐              │              │
    │                  │    │ 10µF  │              │              │
    │                  │    │electro│              │              │
    │                  │    └───┬───┘              │              │
    │                  │        │                  │              │
    │                  │    ┌───┴───┐              │              │
    │                  │    │ 0.1µF │              │              │
    │                  │    │ceramic│              │              │
    │                  │    └───┬───┘              │              │
    │                  │        │                  │              │
    │   Ground ────────┴────────┴──────────────────┴── IC GND     │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Why two capacitors?
    ├── 10µF electrolytic: Filters low-frequency noise (100Hz-100kHz)
    ├── 0.1µF ceramic: Filters high-frequency noise (MHz-GHz)
    └── Place 0.1µF as close as possible to IC power pin!


DECOUPLING RULES:

    ├── Every IC needs at least one 0.1µF capacitor at power pin
    ├── Place capacitor as close as possible to IC ( < 1cm ideal)
    ├── Use ceramic (low ESR, low ESL) for high-frequency
    ├── Use multiple capacitors in parallel for different frequencies
    ├── Bulk capacitor (10-100µF) near power entry point
    └── Route power through capacitor before IC
```

### Filtering (Power Supply Smoothing)

Capacitors smooth the pulsating DC from a rectifier.

```
POWER SUPPLY FILTERING

    AC in ──► Rectifier ──► ┌───┐ ──► DC out
                           ─┤ C ├─
                           ─└───┘─
                              │
    Without capacitor:      With capacitor:
    
    Voltage                 Voltage
        │    ╱╲    ╱╲          │    ────────
        │   ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲         │   ╱        ╲
        │  ╱    ╲╱    ╲        │  ╱          ╲
        │ ╱            ╲       │ ╱            ╲
       0┼╱              ╲──►  0┼╱──────────────╲──► Time
        │                ╲     │                ╲
        │                 ╲    │                 ╲
    
    Pulsating DC (120Hz)    Smooth DC with small ripple


RIPPLE VOLTAGE FORMULA:

    V_ripple = I_load / (2 × f × C)
    
    WHERE:
    V_ripple = peak-to-peak ripple voltage (V)
    I_load = load current (A)
    f = line frequency (50Hz or 60Hz)
    C = filter capacitance (F)
    
    Example: I=1A, f=60Hz, C=1000µF (0.001F)
    V_ripple = 1 / (2 × 60 × 0.001) = 1 / 0.12 = 8.33V
    (too high! need larger capacitor or lower load)
    
    For 1V ripple: C = I / (2 × f × V_ripple) 
                 = 1 / (2 × 60 × 1) = 1 / 120 = 0.0083F = 8300µF
```

### Coupling (DC Blocking)

Capacitors pass AC signals while blocking DC.

```
COUPLING CAPACITOR

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │   Stage 1 (DC=5V, signal=1Vpp)                             │
    │         │                                                  │
    │         │    ┌─────┐                                       │
    │         └────┤  C  ├────┐                                  │
    │              │ 1µF │    │                                  │
    │              └─────┘    │                                  │
    │                         ▼                                  │
    │                    Stage 2 (DC=0V, signal=1Vpp)            │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    What happens:
    ├── DC voltage blocked (capacitor charges to 5V)
    ├── AC signal passes through (voltage changes couple across)
    ├── Output centered around 0V (or bias voltage)
    └── Use: Audio amplifiers, inter-stage coupling


SELECTING COUPLING CAPACITOR:

    Rule: Xc << R_load at lowest frequency of interest
    
    Xc = 1 / (2πfC)
    
    Example: Audio (20Hz), R_load = 10kΩ
    Want Xc < 1kΩ at 20Hz
    C = 1 / (2π × 20 × 1000) = 1 / 125,600 = 8µF
    Use 10µF or larger
```

### Timing (RC Oscillators)

Capacitors and resistors create time delays and oscillations.

```
RC TIMER (555 Oscillator)

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │   5V ──┬────[R1]────┬──────┐                               │
    │        │            │      │                               │
    │        │            │      │  ┌─────────────┐              │
    │        │            │      │  │   NE555     │              │
    │        │            │      │  │             │              │
    │        │            │    ┌─┴─┐│ 7 DISCH     │              │
    │        │            │    │   ││             │              │
    │        │            │    │ C ││ 2 TRIG ─────┼────┐         │
    │        │            │    │   ││             │    │         │
    │        │            │    └─┬─┘│ 6 THRES ────┼────┘         │
    │        │            │      │  │             │              │
    │        │            │      │  │ 3 OUT ──────┼────► Output   │
    │        │            │      │  │             │              │
    │        └────[R2]────┘      │  └─────────────┘              │
    │                           │                                │
    │        └──────────────────┘                                │
    │                                                             │
    │   Frequency = 1.44 / ((R1 + 2×R2) × C)                     │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Example: R1=1kΩ, R2=10kΩ, C=0.1µF
    f = 1.44 / ((1000 + 20000) × 0.0000001)
    f = 1.44 / (21000 × 0.0000001) = 1.44 / 0.0021 = 686 Hz
```

### Energy Storage

Capacitors store energy for quick release.

```
ENERGY STORAGE FORMULA

    E = ½ × C × V²

    WHERE:
    E = energy (joules)
    C = capacitance (farads)
    V = voltage (volts)


EXAMPLE STORED ENERGY:

    Capacitor           Energy
    ──────────────────────────────────────
    1000µF at 5V       0.5 × 0.001 × 25 = 0.0125 J (very small)
    1000µF at 400V     0.5 × 0.001 × 160,000 = 80 J (dangerous!)
    1F at 5V           0.5 × 1 × 25 = 12.5 J
    100F at 2.7V       0.5 × 100 × 7.29 = 365 J (supercapacitor)
    3000F at 2.7V      0.5 × 3000 × 7.29 = 10,935 J (large supercap)


COMPARED TO BATTERIES:

    1 AA battery: ~10,000 J (much more energy)
    But capacitor can release energy much faster!


APPLICATIONS:
├── Camera flash (charge slowly, release quickly)
├── Defibrillator (high energy pulse)
├── Power backup (supercapacitors)
├── Pulsed power (lasers, railguns)
└── Regenerative braking (capture energy)
```

## Measuring Capacitors

### Using a Multimeter with Capacitance Mode

```
CAPACITANCE MEASUREMENT

STEP 1: Power OFF and discharge capacitor!
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  WARNING: Capacitors can hold lethal charge!                │
    │                                                             │
    │  Discharge procedure:                                       │
    │  ├── Use 10kΩ resistor across terminals for 5 seconds      │
    │  ├── For high voltage, use discharge tool                  │
    │  ├── NEVER short with screwdriver (sparks, damage)         │
    │  └── Verify voltage is zero before measuring               │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

STEP 2: Set multimeter to capacitance mode (symbol: ┤├)

STEP 3: Connect leads (polarity matters for electrolytic)

STEP 4: Read value


INTERPRETING RESULTS:

    Within tolerance:     Good (e.g., 100µF ±20% = 80-120µF)
    Low reading:          Dried out electrolyte, failing
    High reading:         Leaky, damaged dielectric
    "OL" or very low:     Open circuit (failed)
    0.00 or very high:    Short circuit (failed)


TESTING WITHOUT CAPACITANCE MODE:

    Resistance mode (for large capacitors):
    ├── Set to highest resistance range
    ├── Connect leads (observe polarity)
    ├── Reading should start low, increase to infinity
    └── If always zero → shorted; always infinite → open
    
    Approximate capacitance from charging time:
    t = 5 × R × C (to full charge)
    Example: R=1MΩ, C=100µF, t=5×1e6×100e-6=500 seconds
```

## Common Capacitor Problems

### Problem 1: Bulging or Leaking Electrolytic

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Top of capacitor bulging (vent opened)
├── Electrolyte leaking (brown crusty residue)
├── Reduced capacitance (measured low)
├── High ESR (circuit malfunctions)

CAUSES:
├── Old age (electrolyte dries out)
├── Overvoltage (exceeded rating)
├── Overheating (poor ventilation)
├── Reverse polarity (installed backwards)
├── Excessive ripple current

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Visual inspection (bulging top, leakage)
├── ESR meter (high resistance)
├── Capacitance meter (low reading)
├── Circuit behavior (noise, instability)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace with same or higher voltage rating
├── Replace with same or higher temperature rating (105°C)
├── Use low-ESR type for switching supplies
├── Improve cooling (ventilation, heatsink)
├── Check polarity before soldering!
```

### Problem 2: Open Capacitor

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── No capacitance (meter reads "OL")
├── Circuit loses filtering (noise, hum)
├── Timing circuit stops working
├── Coupling capacitor blocks all signal

CAUSES:
├── Overvoltage (dielectric breakdown)
├── Physical damage (cracked body)
├── Solder stress (cracked leads)
├── Old age (dry electrolytic)

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Capacitance meter: "OL" or near-zero
├── Resistance meter: infinite (open)
├── In-circuit: no effect (like not there)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace capacitor
├── Use higher voltage rating for reliability
├── Add physical support (glue large caps)
├── Avoid flexing PCB near capacitor
```

### Problem 3: Short Circuit Capacitor

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Meter reads 0Ω or very low resistance
├── Power supply shuts down (overcurrent)
├── Circuit draws excessive current
├── Capacitor gets hot (may explode!)

CAUSES:
├── Overvoltage breakdown
├── Reverse polarity (electrolytic)
├── Manufacturing defect
├── Mechanical damage

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Resistance meter: near 0Ω
├── In-circuit: voltage across cap = 0V
├── Power supply may go into protection

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace capacitor
├── Check for cause (overvoltage, wrong polarity)
├── Use higher voltage rating
├── For tantalum: use 50% voltage derating!
├── Add series protection resistor (if appropriate)
```

### Problem 4: Incorrect Polarity

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Immediate: Capacitor gets very hot
├── Within seconds: Bulging, leaking, explosion
├── Circuit may be damaged
├── Burning smell

CAUSES:
├── Electrolytic installed backwards
├── Tantalum installed backwards
├── PCB marking incorrect
├── Schematic error

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Visual inspection (before power-on!)
├── Measure voltage polarity at pads
├── Compare to capacitor marking

SOLUTIONS:
├── NEVER power on with reversed polarity!
├── Double-check before soldering
├── Use multimeter to verify polarity
├── For new designs: use bipolar/non-polarized caps where possible
├── Mark PCB clearly (+ and -)

POLARITY MARKINGS:

    Aluminum electrolytic:
    ├── Positive lead longer
    ├── Negative side marked with stripe
    ├── Negative marked with minus symbols
    └── Can body is negative

    Tantalum:
    ├── Positive side marked with stripe or plus
    ├── Positive lead longer (some)
    └── Marked end is POSITIVE (opposite of aluminum!)
```

## Summary

1. **Capacitors** store energy in an electric field between two plates

2. **Unit:** Farad (F) = 1 Coulomb per Volt (very large)

3. **Common values:** µF (microfarad), nF (nanofarad), pF (picofarad)

4. **Ceramic capacitors:** Non-polarized, small, cheap, good for high frequency (decoupling)

5. **Electrolytic capacitors:** Polarized, large capacitance, power supply filtering

6. **Tantalum capacitors:** Polarized, stable, FAIL CATASTROPHICALLY (derate 50%!)

7. **Film capacitors:** Non-polarized, low loss, audio/timing applications

8. **Supercapacitors:** Very high capacitance (1F-5000F), energy storage

9. **Time constant:** τ = R × C (63% charge/discharge in 1τ, 99% in 5τ)

10. **Series:** 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ... (decreases capacitance)

11. **Parallel:** C_total = C₁ + C₂ + ... (increases capacitance)

12. **Decoupling:** 0.1µF ceramic + 10-100µF electrolytic at each IC

13. **Voltage rating:** Never exceed! Use 1.5× safety margin (2× for tantalum)

14. **ESR** matters for high-ripple applications (switching supplies)

15. **Failure modes:** Bulging/leaking (electrolytic), open, short, reversed polarity

16. **Always discharge** capacitors before measuring or handling!
