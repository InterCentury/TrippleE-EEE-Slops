# 02.02 - AC Electricity 

## What is Alternating Current?

Alternating Current (AC) is an electric current that periodically reverses direction and continuously changes magnitude. Unlike Direct Current (DC) which flows steadily in one direction, AC alternates back and forth, typically following a sinusoidal waveform.

The development of AC power systems by Nikola Tesla and George Westinghouse in the late 1880s made long-distance electrical distribution practical for the first time. Today, AC is the standard for electrical power transmission and distribution worldwide because it can be easily transformed to different voltages using transformers.

## The Nature of AC Flow

## AC Parameters and Definitions

### Instantaneous, Peak, and Peak-to-Peak Values

```
AC VOLTAGE PARAMETERS

INSTANTANEOUS VOLTAGE (v):
    Voltage at any specific moment in time
    
    Formula: v(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft + φ)
    
    WHERE:
    v(t) = instantaneous voltage at time t
    V_peak = maximum voltage (amplitude)
    f = frequency in Hz
    t = time in seconds
    φ = phase angle (offset)


PEAK VOLTAGE (V_peak or V_p):
    Maximum voltage reached during a cycle
    
    For 120V AC RMS: V_peak = 120 × √2 = 169.7V ≈ 170V
    For 230V AC RMS: V_peak = 230 × √2 = 325.3V ≈ 325V


PEAK-TO-PEAK VOLTAGE (V_pp):
    Voltage from positive peak to negative peak
    
    V_pp = V_peak × 2
    
    For 120V AC: V_pp = 170 × 2 = 340V
    For 230V AC: V_pp = 325 × 2 = 650V


RELATIONSHIPS:

    V_peak = V_RMS × √2
    V_RMS = V_peak / √2
    V_pp = V_peak × 2
    V_pp = V_RMS × 2√2


NUMERICAL EXAMPLE (120V AC system):

    V_RMS = 120V
    V_peak = 120 × 1.414 = 169.7V
    V_pp = 169.7 × 2 = 339.4V ≈ 340V
    
    The voltage swings from +170V to -170V,
    a total change of 340V, 60 times per second!
```

### RMS (Root Mean Square)

RMS is the most important concept for understanding AC. It allows fair comparison with DC.

```
RMS EXPLAINED

WHAT IS RMS?
    RMS is the equivalent DC voltage that produces the same heating effect
    in a resistive load.

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │   120V DC ──► [10Ω heater] ──► Power = 1440W                │
    │                                                             │
    │   120V AC RMS ──► [10Ω heater] ──► Power = 1440W            │
    │                                                             │
    │   Same heating! That's why RMS matters.                     │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘


WHY NOT JUST USE AVERAGE?

    Average voltage of a sine wave over one full cycle = ZERO!
    (positive and negative halves cancel out)
    
    But average of absolute value (rectified average) = 0.637 × V_peak


HOW RMS IS CALCULATED:

    Step 1: Square the voltage at every point (makes all values positive)
    Step 2: Take the mean (average) of all squared values
    Step 3: Take the square root of that mean
    
    Hence: Root → Mean → Square (RMS)


FORMULAS:

    For sine wave:    V_RMS = V_peak / √2 ≈ V_peak × 0.7071
    For square wave:  V_RMS = V_peak (same as peak)
    For triangle:     V_RMS = V_peak / √3 ≈ V_peak × 0.577
    For sawtooth:     V_RMS = V_peak / √3 ≈ V_peak × 0.577


RMS VALUES FOR 170V PEAK (120V RMS system):

    Measurement          Value    Formula
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Peak (V_p)           170V     measured
    Peak-to-Peak (V_pp)  340V     V_p × 2
    RMS                  120V     V_p / √2
    Average (rectified)  108V     V_p × 0.637
    Average (full cycle) 0V       positive + negative cancel


WHY YOUR MULTIMETER READS 120V (not 170V):

    Multimeters are calibrated to display RMS!
    They measure the heating effect (or calculate from peak)
    When you see "120V AC", that's the RMS value.
```

```
    16.67 milliseconds per cycle

    400 Hz (Aircraft, military):
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────►

FREQUENCY EFFECTS:

    Lower frequency (50 Hz):
    ├── Transformers larger/heavier (more iron needed)
    ├── Motors run slower (3000 RPM vs 3600 RPM)

    └── Slightly higher transmission losses

    Higher frequency (60 Hz):
    ├── Transformers smaller/lighter
    ├── Motors run faster
    ├── Less visible flicker
    └── Slightly lower transmission losses

    Higher frequency (400 Hz):
    ├── Much smaller transformers (aerospace advantage)
    ├── Higher core losses (eddy currents, hysteresis)
    ├── More skin effect (current flows on surface)
    └── Used where weight matters (aircraft, ships)
```

### Square Wave

Square waves switch instantly between positive and negative levels.


## AC Phase

Phase describes the timing relationship between two or more AC waveforms.

### Phase Angle

```
PHASE ANGLE EXPLANATION

    Two sine waves with phase difference (φ):

    v1(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft)
    v2(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft + φ)

    WHERE φ = phase angle in degrees or radians


VISUAL EXAMPLE (φ = 90° or π/2 radians):

    Voltage
        │
    1.0 ┼    ╱╲          ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲        ╱  ╲      v1 (0° phase)
        │  ╱    ╲      ╱    ╲
        │ ╱      ╲    ╱      ╲
       0┼╱────────╲──╱────────╲──► Time
        │          ╲╱          ╲
        │           ╲          ╱
        │            ╲        ╱
        │             ╲      ╱      v2 (90° phase lag)
        │              ╲    ╱
        │               ╲  ╱
        │                ╲╱
    -1.0┼
    
    v2 reaches its peak 90° (1/4 cycle) AFTER v1.
    v2 is said to "lag" v1 by 90°.


PHASE RELATIONSHIPS:

    φ = 0° (in phase):
    ╱╲╱╲╱╲    Both peaks align, both zero crossings align
    ╱╲╱╲╱╲

    φ = 90° (quadrature):
     ╱╲    ╱╲    One peaks when other crosses zero
    ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲

    φ = 180° (opposite phase):
     ╱╲    ╱╲    Positive peak aligns with negative peak
    ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲   (inverted)

    φ = 360° (back to in phase):
    Same as 0° (complete cycle)


COMMON PHASE ANGLES:

    Degrees    Radians    Description
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    0°         0          In phase (no difference)
    45°        π/4        One-eighth cycle shift
    90°        π/2        Quadrature (one-quarter cycle)
    120°       2π/3       Three-phase systems
    180°       π          Opposite phase (inverted)
    270°       3π/2       Negative quadrature
    360°       2π         Full cycle (same as 0°)
```

## AC Generation

### Alternators (Synchronous Generators)

Most of the world's electricity is generated by alternators.

```
ALTERNATOR BASICS

    Basic principle: Rotating magnetic field induces AC in stationary coils

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │                      N                                      │
    │                    ┌───┐                                    │
    │                    │   │                                    │
    │                    │   │                                    │
    │              ┌─────┴───┴─────┐                              │
    │              │    Rotor      │                              │
    │              │(electromagnet)│                              │
    │              │      │        │                              │
    │              │      │        │                              │
    │              └──────┼────────┘                              │
    │                     │                                       │
    │    Stator coils ────┼─────────────────────────────          │
    │    (stationary)     │                            │          │
    │                     │                            │          │
    │                     ▼                            │          │
    │              ┌─────────────┐                     │          │
    │              │   AC output │                     │          │
    │              └─────────────┘                     │          │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Operation:
    ├── Rotor (field) rotates inside stator
    ├── DC applied to rotor windings (via slip rings)
    ├── Rotating magnetic field induces voltage in stator coils
    ├── Output frequency = (RPM × poles) / 120
    └── Voltage regulated by controlling rotor DC current


FREQUENCY CALCULATION:

    f = (RPM × P) / 120

    WHERE:
    f = frequency in Hz
    RPM = rotational speed
    P = number of magnetic poles (even number)


EXAMPLES:

    2-pole generator:
    3600 RPM → f = (3600 × 2) / 120 = 7200 / 120 = 60 Hz
    3000 RPM → f = (3000 × 2) / 120 = 6000 / 120 = 50 Hz

    4-pole generator:
    1800 RPM → f = (1800 × 4) / 120 = 7200 / 120 = 60 Hz
    1500 RPM → f = (1500 × 4) / 120 = 6000 / 120 = 50 Hz

    6-pole generator:
    1200 RPM → f = (1200 × 6) / 120 = 7200 / 120 = 60 Hz
```

## AC Power

### Instantaneous Power

In AC circuits, power varies continuously throughout the cycle.

```
INSTANTANEOUS AC POWER

    For a resistive load (heater, incandescent bulb):
    
    p(t) = v(t) × i(t)
    
    v(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft)
    i(t) = I_peak × sin(2πft)  (current in phase with voltage)
    
    p(t) = V_peak × I_peak × sin²(2πft)


    Power waveform (resistive load):
    Power
        │    ╱╲    ╱╲
        │   ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
        │  ╱    ╲╱    ╲
        │ ╱            ╲
       0┼╱──────────────╲──► Time
        │                ╲
        │                 ╲
    
    Power pulses at twice the line frequency (120 Hz for 60 Hz AC)
    Power never negative (resistor always consumes, never returns)


    For a reactive load (motor with inductor):
    
    v(t) = V_peak × sin(2πft)
    i(t) = I_peak × sin(2πft - φ)  (current lags voltage)
    
    p(t) = V_peak × I_peak × sin(2πft) × sin(2πft - φ)


    Power waveform (inductive load):
    Power
        │     ╱╲
        │    ╱  ╲
        │   ╱    ╲
        │  ╱      ╲
       0┼ ╱────────╲─────► Time
        │╱        ╲
        │          ╲╱
        │           ╲
        │            ╲
    
    Power becomes negative during part of cycle!
    (Energy stored in inductor returned to source)
```

### Real, Reactive, and Apparent Power

```
AC POWER TRIANGLE

                  S (Apparent Power - VA)
                 /|
                / |
               /  | Q (Reactive Power - VAR)
              /   |
             /    |
            / φ   |
           /______|
           P (Real Power - Watts)


DEFINITIONS:

    Real Power (P) - Watts (W):
    ├── Actual power that does work
    ├── Heats resistors, lights bulbs, turns motors
    ├── P = V_RMS × I_RMS × cos(φ)
    └── What you pay for on electric bill

    Reactive Power (Q) - Volt-Amperes Reactive (VAR):
    ├── Power stored and returned by inductors/capacitors
    ├── Does no useful work
    ├── Q = V_RMS × I_RMS × sin(φ)
    └── Causes extra current in wires (losses)

    Apparent Power (S) - Volt-Amperes (VA):
    ├── Vector sum of P and Q
    ├── What utilities must supply
    ├── S = V_RMS × I_RMS
    └── S² = P² + Q²


POWER FACTOR (PF):

    PF = P / S = cos(φ)

    PF = 1.0 (perfect): All power is real (resistive load)
    PF = 0.8 (typical): 80% real, 20% reactive (motor)
    PF = 0.0 (worst): All power is reactive (pure L or C)


NUMERICAL EXAMPLE:

    Motor: 230V, 5A, PF = 0.8 lagging
    
    S = 230 × 5 = 1150 VA
    P = 1150 × 0.8 = 920 W
    Q = √(1150² - 920²) = √(1,322,500 - 846,400) = √476,100 = 690 VAR
    
    The motor uses 920W of real power (what you pay for)
    but draws 1150VA from the grid (what the utility must supply).


WHY POWER FACTOR MATTERS:

    Poor PF = Higher current for same real power:
    
    1000W load at 230V:
    PF = 1.0: I = 1000 / 230 = 4.35A
    PF = 0.7: I = 1000 / (230 × 0.7) = 6.21A (43% higher!)
    
    Higher current means:
    ├── Larger wires needed
    ├── Higher I²R losses
    ├── More voltage drop
    └── Utilities may charge penalty fees
```

## AC Effects on Components

### Resistors in AC

Resistors behave the same in AC as DC (for most frequencies).

```
RESISTOR AC BEHAVIOR

    Ideal resistor:
    ├── Resistance same at all frequencies (DC to GHz)
    ├── Voltage and current in phase (φ = 0°)
    ├── Power factor = 1.0 (perfect)
    └── Power = V_RMS × I_RMS = I²R = V²/R

    Real resistor (high frequency effects):
    ├── At very high frequencies (>100 MHz), parasitic capacitance matters
    ├── Wirewound resistors have inductance
    ├── Carbon composition has less inductance than wirewound
    ├── Surface mount resistors best for RF
    └── For power frequencies (50/60 Hz), resistors are ideal


    PHASOR DIAGRAM (Resistor):

    Voltage (V) ────────► (0°)
    Current (I) ────────► (0°)
    
    V and I are perfectly aligned (in phase)
```

### Capacitors in AC

Capacitors oppose changes in voltage. In AC, they pass current but with phase shift.

```
CAPACITOR AC BEHAVIOR

    Capacitive reactance: Xc = 1 / (2πfC)
    
    WHERE:
    Xc = capacitive reactance (Ω) - opposition to AC
    f = frequency (Hz)
    C = capacitance (F)


    FREQUENCY EFFECT:

    f = 0 (DC):      Xc = ∞ (open circuit)
    f = 60 Hz:       Xc = 1/(2π×60×C)
    f = 1 kHz:       Xc = 1/(2π×1000×C)  (1/16.7 × 60 Hz value)
    f = ∞:           Xc = 0 (short circuit)


    NUMERICAL EXAMPLE (C = 10µF):

    f = 60 Hz:   Xc = 1/(2π×60×0.00001) = 1/0.00377 = 265Ω
    f = 120 Hz:  Xc = 1/(2π×120×0.00001) = 1/0.00754 = 133Ω
    f = 1 kHz:   Xc = 1/(2π×1000×0.00001) = 1/0.0628 = 15.9Ω
    f = 10 kHz:  Xc = 1/(2π×10000×0.00001) = 1/0.628 = 1.59Ω


    PHASE RELATIONSHIP:

    Current LEADS voltage by 90° (in a pure capacitor)
    
    Voltage:     ╱╲    ╱╲
               ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
              ╱    ╲╱    ╲
    Current:   ╱╲    ╱╲
             ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
            ╱    ╲╱    ╲


    PHASOR DIAGRAM (Capacitor):

    Current (I) ──► (90° leading)
         │
         │ 90°
         │
         ▼
    Voltage (V) ──► (0°)

    Current peaks 90° BEFORE voltage


    POWER IN CAPACITOR:

    Average power = 0 (ideal capacitor stores and returns all energy)
    But capacitor draws current = source must supply reactive power (VAR)
```

### Inductors in AC

Inductors oppose changes in current. In AC, they also pass current with phase shift.

```
INDUCTOR AC BEHAVIOR

    Inductive reactance: XL = 2πfL
    
    WHERE:
    XL = inductive reactance (Ω) - opposition to AC
    f = frequency (Hz)
    L = inductance (H)


    FREQUENCY EFFECT:

    f = 0 (DC):      XL = 0 (short circuit)
    f = 60 Hz:       XL = 2π×60×L
    f = 1 kHz:       XL = 2π×1000×L  (16.7× higher than 60 Hz)
    f = ∞:           XL = ∞ (open circuit)


    NUMERICAL EXAMPLE (L = 10mH):

    f = 60 Hz:   XL = 2π×60×0.01 = 3.77Ω
    f = 120 Hz:  XL = 2π×120×0.01 = 7.54Ω
    f = 1 kHz:   XL = 2π×1000×0.01 = 62.8Ω
    f = 10 kHz:  XL = 2π×10000×0.01 = 628Ω


    PHASE RELATIONSHIP:

    Current LAGS voltage by 90° (in a pure inductor)
    
    Voltage:     ╱╲    ╱╲
               ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
              ╱    ╲╱    ╲
    Current:      ╱╲    ╱╲
                ╱  ╲  ╱  ╲
               ╱    ╲╱    ╲


    PHASOR DIAGRAM (Inductor):

    Voltage (V) ──► (0°)
         │
         │ 90°
         │
         ▼
    Current (I) ──► (-90° lagging)

    Current peaks 90° AFTER voltage


    POWER IN INDUCTOR:

    Average power = 0 (ideal inductor stores and returns all energy)
    But inductor draws current = source must supply reactive power (VAR)
```

## AC vs DC: Why AC Won (and Why DC is Coming Back)

```
THE GREAT DEBATE: AC vs DC

WHY AC WON (1880s-1890s):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    1. TRANSFORMERS:
       ├── AC can be stepped up/down easily
       ├── High voltage = low current = low losses
       ├── 100kV for transmission, 120V for homes
       └── DC cannot use transformers (needs complex converters)

    2. LONG DISTANCE:
       ├── AC transmission losses much lower
       ├── 100 miles possible with AC
       ├── 1 mile limit for DC (1880s technology)
       └── AC enabled rural electrification

    3. MOTORS:
       ├── AC induction motor (Tesla) simple and rugged
       ├── No brushes (no wear, no sparks)
       ├── Constant speed regardless of load (approx)
       └── Ideal for factories and appliances

    4. COST:
       ├── AC generators cheaper than DC
       ├── No commutator (slip rings instead)
       ├── Transformers cheap and efficient
       └── AC distribution won economically


WHY DC IS COMING BACK (21st Century):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    1. ELECTRONICS:
       ├── All electronics run on DC
       ├── Every device needs AC→DC conversion
       ├── Conversion losses = heat
       └── DC distribution could save 10-20% energy

    2. RENEWABLE ENERGY:
       ├── Solar panels produce DC
       ├── Batteries store DC
       ├── EVs use DC
       └── Converting to AC loses energy

    3. HVDC TRANSMISSION:
       ├── HVDC now practical (power electronics)
       ├── Lower losses than AC for long distance
       ├── No skin effect (uses full conductor)
       ├── Can connect asynchronous grids
       └── Used for undersea cables, long lines

    4. DATA CENTERS:
       ├── Servers run on DC (12V, 48V)
       ├── AC→DC conversion losses significant
       ├── DC distribution saves 10-15%
       └── Google, Facebook, Microsoft using DC


MODERN COMPROMISE (Best of Both):

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                             │
    │   Generation: AC (turbines, alternators)                    │
    │        │                                                    │
    │        ▼                                                    │
    │   Transmission: HVDC for long, HVAC for medium             │
    │        │                                                    │
    │        ▼                                                    │
    │   Distribution: AC (transformer to 120V/230V)              │
    │        │                                                    │
    │        ▼                                                    │
    │   End use: Convert to DC (rectifier) for electronics        │
    │                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Future trend: More DC distribution
    ├── USB-C (up to 240W at 48V)
    ├── PoE (Power over Ethernet, 48V)
    ├── DC microgrids (solar + batteries)
    └── Homes with DC bus (48V for lights, USB)
```

## Summary

1. **Alternating Current (AC)** periodically reverses direction and changes magnitude

2. **Sine wave** is the natural AC waveform (smooth, efficient, low harmonics)

3. **RMS (Root Mean Square)** allows fair comparison with DC: 120V AC RMS = 120V DC for heating

4. **Peak voltage** = V_RMS × √2 (170V for 120V system, 325V for 230V system)

5. **Frequency** = 50 Hz (Europe/Asia) or 60 Hz (Americas); period T = 1/f

6. **Phase** describes timing between waveforms (0° in phase, 90° quadrature, 180° opposite)

7. **Three-phase AC** provides constant power, self-starting motors, more efficient transmission

8. **AC generation:** Alternators (synchronous generators) produce sine waves; inverters convert DC to AC

9. **AC power types:** Real (watts), Reactive (VAR), Apparent (VA); Power factor = P/S

10. **Capacitors** pass high frequencies (Xc ∝ 1/f), current leads voltage by 90°

11. **Inductors** pass low frequencies (XL ∝ f), current lags voltage by 90°

12. **AC won the War of Currents** due to transformers (enabling long-distance transmission)

13. **DC is returning** for HVDC transmission, renewable energy, data centers, and electronics
