# 02.06 - AC vs DC: Efficiency and Use Cases

## The Great Debate: Which is Better?

Neither AC nor DC is universally "better." Each has distinct advantages and disadvantages that make it suitable for different applications. The choice depends on distance, power level, cost, safety requirements, and the specific devices being powered.

Understanding when to use AC versus DC is essential for designing efficient electrical systems. This document explores the efficiency considerations and optimal use cases for both AC and DC power.

## Efficiency Comparison: AC vs DC

### Transmission Efficiency

The most significant difference between AC and DC is how efficiently they transmit power over distance.

```
TRANSMISSION EFFICIENCY COMPARISON

POWER LOSS FORMULA (Both AC and DC):

    P_loss = I² × R

WHERE:
P_loss = power lost as heat (watts)
I = current (amperes)
R = resistance of transmission line (ohms)

KEY INSIGHT: To reduce losses, REDUCE CURRENT!

Since P = V × I, for the same power:
    Higher voltage = Lower current = Lower losses


AC TRANSMISSION:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Advantages:
    ├── Easy voltage transformation (transformers)
    ├── Step up to very high voltage (765kV+)
    ├── Lower current for given power
    ├── Mature technology (100+ years)
    └── Lower cost for medium distances

    Disadvantages:
    ├── Skin effect (current flows on surface, increases effective resistance)
    ├── Reactive power losses (power factor)
    ├── Corona discharge at very high voltages
    ├── Requires synchronization for grid connection
    └── Higher losses than DC for very long distances

    Typical efficiency: 95-98% per 100km at 500kV


DC TRANSMISSION (HVDC):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Advantages:
    ├── No skin effect (uses full conductor cross-section)
    ├── No reactive power losses (power factor = 1.0)
    ├── Lower losses than AC for long distances (>500km)
    ├── Can connect asynchronous grids (different frequencies)
    ├── No synchronization required
    └── Better for undersea cables (no charging current)

    Disadvantages:
    ├── Expensive converters at each end (AC↔DC)
    ├── High cost for short distances
    ├── Complex technology
    └── Less mature than AC (but rapidly improving)

    Typical efficiency: 96-98% per 1000km at ±500kV (better than AC!)


BREAK-EVEN DISTANCE:

    Loss (%)
        │
    10% ┼──────────────────────────────────────
        │                                   ╱
     8% ┼──────────────────────────────────╱
        │                                ╱
     6% ┼──────────────────────────────╱
        │                            ╱
     4% ┼──────────────────────────╱
        │                        ╱
     2% ┼────────────────────╱
        │                   ╱
     0% ┼──────────────────╱────────────────► Distance
        0   200   400   600   800   1000   1200 km
        
        AC losses ────  DC losses ────
        
    For distances < 500km: AC is more economical
    For distances > 800km: DC has lower losses
    Break-even: approximately 600-800km
```

### Distribution Efficiency

Once power reaches populated areas, distribution efficiency depends on voltage level.

```
DISTRIBUTION VOLTAGE COMPARISON

VOLTAGE        TYPICAL LOSSES    BEST USE CASE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
765kV AC       ~1% per 200km     Long-distance transmission
500kV AC       ~2% per 200km     Regional transmission
138kV AC       ~3% per 100km     Sub-transmission
13.8kV AC      ~5% per 10km      Primary distribution
480V AC        ~3% per 500m      Industrial (short runs)
240V AC        ~4% per 100m      Residential (US)
230V AC        ~4% per 100m      Residential (EU)
120V AC        ~6% per 50m       Residential outlets (US)
48V DC         ~3% per 50m       Telecom, data centers
24V DC         ~5% per 20m       Industrial control
12V DC         ~10% per 10m      Automotive, LED strips
5V DC          ~20% per 5m       USB, electronics


LOSS CALCULATION EXAMPLE:

    100W load, 10 meters of 18 AWG copper wire (0.021Ω/m)
    
    AC 120V:
    I = P/V = 100/120 = 0.833A
    R_total = 20m × 0.021 = 0.42Ω (round trip)
    P_loss = 0.833² × 0.42 = 0.29W (0.29% loss)
    
    DC 12V:
    I = P/V = 100/12 = 8.33A
    R_total = 20m × 0.021 = 0.42Ω
    P_loss = 8.33² × 0.42 = 29.2W (29.2% loss!)
    
    CONCLUSION: Higher voltage dramatically reduces losses!
```

### Conversion Efficiency

Every time power changes form (AC↔DC), energy is lost.

```
CONVERSION EFFICIENCIES

AC TO DC CONVERSION (Rectification):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Linear power supply:
    ├── Efficiency: 40-60%
    ├── Loss: 40-60% as heat
    └── Used in: Audio, lab supplies, low-noise applications

    Switching power supply (SMPS):
    ├── Efficiency: 70-95%
    ├── Loss: 5-30% as heat
    └── Used in: Most consumer electronics

    High-quality SMPS (80 PLUS Titanium):
    ├── Efficiency: 94-96%
    ├── Loss: 4-6%
    └── Used in: Servers, high-end computers

    Best-in-class (resonant converters):
    ├── Efficiency: 97-99%
    ├── Loss: 1-3%
    └── Used in: Telecom rectifiers, EV chargers


DC TO AC CONVERSION (Inversion):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Modified sine wave inverter:
    ├── Efficiency: 80-90%
    ├── Loss: 10-20%
    └── Used in: Basic backup power, tools

    Pure sine wave inverter:
    ├── Efficiency: 85-95%
    ├── Loss: 5-15%
    └── Used in: Solar systems, RVs, sensitive equipment

    Grid-tie inverter (high quality):
    ├── Efficiency: 96-98.5%
    ├── Loss: 1.5-4%
    └── Used in: Solar panel systems


DC TO DC CONVERSION (Voltage change):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Linear regulator (step-down only):
    ├── Efficiency: V_out / V_in × 100% (very poor for large drops)
    ├── Example: 12V→5V = 41.7% efficient!
    └── Used in: Low-current, low-noise applications

    Buck converter (step-down):
    ├── Efficiency: 80-95%
    ├── Loss: 5-20%
    └── Used in: Most DC-DC applications

    Boost converter (step-up):
    ├── Efficiency: 80-93%
    ├── Loss: 7-20%
    └── Used in: Battery-powered LED drivers

    Buck-boost (step-up/down):
    ├── Efficiency: 75-90%
    ├── Loss: 10-25%
    └── Used in: Battery-powered devices


AC TO AC CONVERSION (Transformer):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Small transformer (<50VA):
    ├── Efficiency: 70-85%
    ├── Loss: 15-30%
    └── Used in: Wall warts, doorbells

    Medium transformer (50-500VA):
    ├── Efficiency: 85-95%
    ├── Loss: 5-15%
    └── Used in: Power supplies, audio

    Large transformer (>1MVA):
    ├── Efficiency: 98-99.5%
    ├── Loss: 0.5-2%
    └── Used in: Power grid substations
```

## Use Case Analysis

### When AC is Superior

```
AC USE CASES

1. LONG-DISTANCE POWER TRANSMISSION (Medium distance):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why AC wins:
    ├── Transformers are simple, cheap, efficient
    ├── Can step up to very high voltages (reduce losses)
    ├── Mature technology, widely available
    └── Lower initial cost than HVDC for <500km

    Examples:
    ├── Power grid backbone (hundreds of km)
    ├── Regional transmission networks
    └── Interconnecting power plants to substations


2. ELECTRIC MOTORS (Certain types):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    AC induction motor advantages:
    ├── No brushes (maintenance-free)
    ├── Simple, rugged construction
    ├── Low cost
    ├── Constant speed (synchronous or near-synchronous)
    └── High efficiency (85-95% for large motors)

    Examples:
    ├── Industrial pumps, fans, compressors
    ├── HVAC systems
    ├── Washing machines, dryers
    └── Electric vehicles (some use AC motors with inverters)


3. RESIDENTIAL AND COMMERCIAL BUILDINGS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why AC is standard for buildings:
    ├── Existing infrastructure (100+ years of deployment)
    ├── Easy voltage transformation (120V/240V/480V)
    ├── Compatible with all standard appliances
    ├── Circuit breakers work well with AC (zero crossing)
    └── Code requirements (NEC, IEC based on AC)

    Examples:
    ├── Wall outlets (120V US, 230V EU)
    ├── Lighting circuits
    ├── HVAC systems
    └── Major appliances (ovens, dryers, water heaters)


4. TRANSFORMER-COUPLED SYSTEMS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why AC is required:
    ├── Transformers work ONLY with AC (changing magnetic field)
    ├── Isolation transformers provide safety
    ├── Impedance matching for maximum power transfer
    └── Voltage scaling for different requirements

    Examples:
    ├── Isolation for medical equipment
    ├── Matching speakers to amplifiers (audio transformers)
    ├── Impedance matching in RF circuits
    └── Voltage conversion in power supplies (before rectification)
```

### When DC is Superior

```
DC USE CASES

1. LONG-DISTANCE POWER TRANSMISSION (Very long distance):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why HVDC wins for >800km:
    ├── No skin effect (uses full conductor)
    ├── No reactive power losses
    ├── Lower losses than AC for long distances
    ├── Can connect asynchronous grids (50Hz to 60Hz)
    └── Better for undersea cables (no charging current)

    Examples:
    ├── Undersea cables (e.g., North Sea wind farms)
    ├── Cross-country transmission (China, Brazil, Russia)
    ├── Connecting different frequency grids (Japan: 50Hz/60Hz)
    └── Remote hydroelectric plants (e.g., Quebec to New England)


2. ALL ELECTRONICS AND DIGITAL DEVICES:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why electronics need DC:
    ├── Transistors and ICs require DC (logic levels)
    ├── Microcontrollers, CPUs, memory all use DC
    ├── LEDs are DC devices
    ├── LCD/LED displays require DC
    └── Almost every electronic component runs on DC

    Examples:
    ├── Smartphones, tablets, laptops
    ├── Desktop computers (internal DC, external AC→DC)
    ├── TVs, monitors, radios
    ├── Routers, modems, network switches
    └── All battery-powered devices


3. RENEWABLE ENERGY AND STORAGE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why DC is natural for renewables:
    ├── Solar panels produce DC directly
    ├── Batteries store DC
    ├── Fuel cells produce DC
    ├── EV batteries are DC (400V-800V)
    └── Converting to AC loses energy (3-10%)

    Examples:
    ├── Solar panels → DC microgrid
    ├── Battery energy storage systems
    ├── Electric vehicle charging (DC fast charging)
    ├── Off-grid systems (solar + batteries)
    └── DC-coupled solar + storage (more efficient)


4. DATA CENTERS AND TELECOM:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why DC distribution is gaining:
    ├── Servers run on DC internally (12V, 48V)
    ├── AC→DC conversion in each server = multiple losses
    ├── Central DC distribution eliminates multiple conversions
    ├── 10-20% energy savings reported
    └── Reduced cooling requirements (less heat from conversions)

    Examples:
    ├── Google data centers (48V DC distribution)
    ├── Facebook data centers (277V AC or 48V DC)
    ├── Telecom central offices (-48V DC standard)
    ├── Edge computing nodes
    └── Colocation facilities with DC options


5. ELECTRIC VEHICLES (EVs):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Why EV powertrains use DC:
    ├── Battery packs are DC (400V, 800V typical)
    ├── Motors are AC (but driven by DC→AC inverter)
    ├── Traction battery stores DC
    ├── Regenerative braking generates DC (rectified from motor)
    └── DC fast charging (CCS, CHAdeMO, Tesla Supercharger)

    Examples:
    ├── Tesla Model 3: 350V DC battery, AC induction motor
    ├── Nissan Leaf: 400V DC battery, AC permanent magnet motor
    ├── Porsche Taycan: 800V DC battery (faster charging)
    └── E-bikes, e-scooters: lower voltage DC (36V-72V)


6. DC MICROGRIDS AND BUILDING DISTRIBUTION:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Emerging trend: DC within buildings
    ├── LED lighting: native DC (12V, 24V, 48V)
    ├── USB-C: 5V-48V DC, up to 240W
    ├── Fans, small appliances: can run on DC
    ├── Solar + battery systems: already DC
    └── Eliminate multiple AC→DC conversions

    Examples:
    ├── EMerge Alliance standard (24V DC, 48V DC)
    ├── USB-C powered monitors and laptops
    ├── DC-powered ceiling lights (48V)
    └── Whole-home DC microgrids (experimental)
```

## End-to-End Efficiency: The Big Picture

The true efficiency of a system depends on ALL conversions from source to load.

```
EFFICIENCY CHAIN ANALYSIS

EXAMPLE 1: TRADITIONAL AC GRID TO LED BULB
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Power plant (40% efficient) → AC grid (95%) → 
    Building transformer (98%) → Wall outlet → 
    LED driver (AC→DC, 85%) → LED bulb (30% efficient)
    
    TOTAL EFFICIENCY = 0.40 × 0.95 × 0.98 × 0.85 × 0.30 = 9.5%
    
    Only 9.5% of fuel energy becomes light!


EXAMPLE 2: SOLAR DC TO LED BULB (DC MICROGRID)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Solar panel (20% efficient) → DC microgrid (98%) → 
    LED bulb (30% efficient)
    
    TOTAL EFFICIENCY = 0.20 × 0.98 × 0.30 = 5.9%
    
    Wait, that's lower! But fuel is free (sun).
    Also eliminates plant, transmission, transformer, driver losses.


EXAMPLE 3: SOLAR DC + BATTERY TO LED (OFF-GRID)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Solar panel (20%) → Charge controller (95%) → 
    Battery (85% round trip) → LED driver (95%) → LED (30%)
    
    TOTAL EFFICIENCY = 0.20 × 0.95 × 0.85 × 0.95 × 0.30 = 4.6%
    
    Battery storage adds losses but enables 24/7 operation.


EXAMPLE 4: AC GRID TO COMPUTER (TRADITIONAL)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Power plant (40%) → AC grid (95%) → Building transformer (98%) →
    Wall outlet → PSU (AC→DC, 80%) → VRMs (DC→DC, 90%) → CPU (variable)
    
    TOTAL EFFICIENCY = 0.40 × 0.95 × 0.98 × 0.80 × 0.90 = 26.8%
    
    Plus CPU efficiency (not included in this calculation)


EXAMPLE 5: DC DISTRIBUTION IN DATA CENTER
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Power plant (40%) → AC grid (95%) → Building transformer (98%) →
    AC→DC rectifier (96%) → 48V DC bus (99%) → 
    Server DC-DC converter (95%) → CPU
    
    TOTAL EFFICIENCY = 0.40 × 0.95 × 0.98 × 0.96 × 0.99 × 0.95 = 33.7%
    
    Better than traditional PSU per-server conversion (26.8%)!
    Eliminates: each server's AC-DC PSU loss (replaced by central rectifier)
```

## Efficiency Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Raise Voltage

```
VOLTAGE VS EFFICIENCY

    Power = 100W, Distance = 10m, 18 AWG wire
    
    Voltage    Current    Loss       Efficiency
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    5V         20A        168W       37% (impossible!)
    12V        8.33A      29.2W      70%
    24V        4.17A      7.3W       93%
    48V        2.08A      1.8W       98%
    120V       0.83A      0.29W      99.7%
    
    Rule: Double voltage = quarter the losses!
    Rule: Higher voltage always more efficient for same power


PRACTICAL VOLTAGE SELECTION:

    Application                    Recommended Voltage
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    USB devices                    5V (standard) or USB-PD (negotiated)
    LED strips (short run)         12V
    LED strips (long run)          24V or 48V
    Small electronics              12V, 24V
    Telecom equipment              48V (-48V)
    Data centers                   48V, 380V
    Industrial control             24V
    EV auxiliary systems           12V, 48V
    EV traction battery            400V, 800V
    Solar panel strings            300V-600V (grid-tie), 48V (off-grid)
```

### Strategy 2: Minimize Conversions

```
CONVERSION COUNT MATTERS

    Each conversion adds 2-10% loss.
    Minimize number of AC↔DC conversions!


POOR DESIGN (Many conversions):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Solar (DC) → Inverter (DC→AC, 90%) → AC Grid (95%) → 
    Building AC (98%) → Phone Charger (AC→DC, 85%) → 
    Phone Battery (DC, 90% charging) → Phone (90%)
    
    TOTAL = 0.90 × 0.95 × 0.98 × 0.85 × 0.90 × 0.90 = 55% loss!
    
    Only 45% of solar energy reaches phone.


BETTER DESIGN (Fewer conversions):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Solar (DC) → Charge Controller (DC→DC, 95%) → 
    Battery (90%) → Phone (90%)
    
    TOTAL = 0.95 × 0.90 × 0.90 = 77% efficient (only 23% loss)
    
    Direct DC coupling eliminates inverter and AC charger losses!
```

## Summary

1. **Neither AC nor DC is universally better** - each excels in different applications

2. **AC advantages:** Easy voltage transformation (transformers), long-distance transmission (medium distance), induction motors, existing infrastructure

3. **DC advantages:** Electronics compatibility, renewable energy integration, battery storage, HVDC for very long distances (>800km)

4. **Transmission efficiency:** AC loses 2-5% per 100km at 500kV; DC loses 2-4% per 1000km at ±500kV

5. **Break-even distance:** HVDC becomes more economical than AC beyond 600-800km

6. **Conversion efficiency:** AC→DC (70-99%), DC→AC (80-98%), DC→DC (80-95%), AC→AC (70-99%)

7. **Higher voltage = lower losses** (double voltage = quarter the losses)

8. **Each conversion adds 2-10% loss** - minimize number of conversions

9. **End-to-end efficiency** from fuel to light can be as low as 5-10% (traditional) or 15-30% (optimized)

10. **Data centers** can save 10-20% with DC distribution (48V or 380V)

11. **Hybrid systems** use AC for transmission, DC for renewables, storage, and electronics

12. **Future trends:** GaN/SiC converters (98-99% efficient), USB-C universal power, DC microgrids, HVDC super grids
