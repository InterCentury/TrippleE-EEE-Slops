# 01.08 - AC vs DC

## What are AC and DC?

AC (Alternating Current) and DC (Direct Current) are the two fundamental types of electric current. They differ in how the flow of electrons behaves over time.

- **DC** flows in one direction only, with constant polarity
- **AC** periodically reverses direction, alternating between positive and negative

The choice between AC and DC has shaped the entire electrical infrastructure of the modern world. In the late 19th century, a "War of Currents" was fought between Thomas Edison (promoting DC) and Nikola Tesla/George Westinghouse (promoting AC). AC won for power distribution, but DC remains essential for electronics.

### Characteristics of AC

```

    Voltage (V)
        │
    +170┼      ╱╲
        │     ╱  ╲
        │    ╱    ╲

    KEY FEATURES:
    ├── Reverses polarity (positive to negative)
    ├── Magnitude changes continuously
    ├── Frequency = cycles per second (Hz)
    ├── Period = time for one cycle (1/f)
    └── Peak voltage = 170V for 120V RMS


AC PARAMETERS:

    Peak Voltage (V_peak)    = Maximum voltage (170V for 120V AC)
    Peak-to-Peak (V_pp)      = V_peak × 2 (340V for 120V AC)
    RMS (Root Mean Square)   = V_peak / √2 (120V for 170V peak)
    Average Voltage          = V_peak × 0.637 (for full-wave rectified)
    Frequency (f)            = 50 Hz or 60 Hz
    Period (T)               = 1 / f (16.67 ms at 60 Hz, 20 ms at 50 Hz)
    
    FORMULAS:
    V_peak = V_RMS × √2
    V_RMS = V_peak / √2
    V_peak = V_pp / 2
```

## RMS: The Key to Understanding AC

RMS (Root Mean Square) is how we compare AC to DC. An AC voltage's RMS value produces the same heating effect as the same DC voltage.

```
RMS EXPLANATION

THE HEATING COMPARISON:

    DC: 120V ──► [10Ω heater] ──► Heat = 1440W
    
    AC: 120V_RMS ──► [10Ω heater] ──► Heat = 1440W
    
    The AC peaks are higher (170V), but the average heating
    matches 120V DC.


WHY RMS MATTERS:

    When you say "120V AC" you mean 120V RMS!
    
    V_peak = 120 × √2 = 120 × 1.414 = 170V
    V_pp = 170 × 2 = 340V


NUMERICAL EXAMPLE:

    AC sine wave with V_peak = 170V:
    
    Instantaneous power at any moment:
    P_instant = V_instant² / R
    
    Average power over one cycle:
    P_avg = (V_peak² / 2) / R = (V_peak/√2)² / R
    
    So V_RMS = V_peak / √2 = 170 / 1.414 = 120V


RMS VALUES FOR DIFFERENT WAVEFORMS:

    Waveform          V_RMS (relative to V_peak)
    ────────────────────────────────────────────
    Sine wave         V_peak / √2 = 0.707 × V_peak
    Square wave       V_peak (same as peak)
    Triangle wave     V_peak / √3 = 0.577 × V_peak
    Sawtooth wave     V_peak / √3 = 0.577 × V_peak
```

## The War of Currents (Historical Context)

```
THE GREAT AC/DC BATTLE (1880s-1890s)

THOMAS EDISON (DC Champion):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    ├── Invented practical light bulb (1879)
    ├── Built first DC power station (Pearl Street, 1882)
    ├── DC was first, had momentum
    ├── Argued AC was dangerous (electrocuted animals publicly)
    ├── Pushed for AC to be used in electric chair (to scare public)
    └── Problem: DC couldn't go more than 1 mile from plant


NIKOLA TESLA / GEORGE WESTINGHOUSE (AC Champions):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    ├── Tesla invented AC induction motor (1883)
    ├── Westinghouse bought Tesla's patents
    ├── AC can be stepped up/down with transformers
    ├── High voltage = low current = low losses = long distance
    ├── Won contract for Niagara Falls hydro plant (1893)
    └── AC became world standard


WHY AC WON:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    1. Transformers (step up for transmission, step down for use)
    2. Long-distance capability (miles vs hundreds of miles)
    3. AC induction motors (simple, rugged, no brushes)
    4. Economics (cheaper to build and operate)
    5. Scalability (can serve entire cities, then regions)


WHY DC DIDN'T DIE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    1. Electronics need DC (transistors, ICs)
    2. Batteries store DC (portable devices)
    3. Solar panels produce DC
    4. EVs use DC (but charge from AC)
    5. HVDC for ultra-long transmission (now making comeback!)


MODERN TRUCE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    ├── Grid: AC for distribution (120V/230V to homes)
    ├── Transmission: AC for medium, HVDC for long
    ├── Homes: AC at outlets
    ├── Devices: Convert AC to DC internally
    └── Best of both worlds!
```

## Safety Considerations

### AC vs DC Danger

```
ELECTRICAL SAFETY COMPARISON

AC (60 Hz) is more dangerous than DC at the same voltage:

WHY AC IS MORE DANGEROUS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    1. Causes ventricular fibrillation (heart stops pumping)
    2. Alternating current causes muscle contractions that prevent letting go
    3. 60 Hz is close to heart's natural rhythm (interferes more)
    4. Lower threshold for "can't let go" (10-15 mA AC vs 50-75 mA DC)


SAFETY THRESHOLDS (60 Hz AC vs DC):

    Effect                    AC (60 Hz)         DC
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Perception threshold      0.5-1 mA           5-10 mA
    Painful shock             3-5 mA             10-20 mA
    "Can't let go"            10-15 mA           50-75 mA
    Breathing difficulty      15-30 mA           50-100 mA
    Ventricular fibrillation  50-100 mA          300-500 mA
    Cardiac arrest            100-200 mA         >500 mA
    Severe burns              >200 mA            >500 mA


VOLTAGE COMPARISON (Lethality):

    ├── 120V AC (wall outlet) → CAN KILL (especially if wet)
    ├── 230V AC (EU outlet)   → WILL KILL (very dangerous)
    ├── 12V DC (car battery)  → Safe (can't penetrate skin)
    ├── 48V DC (telecom)      → Safe (dry skin)
    └── 300V+ DC              → As dangerous as AC


PRACTICAL SAFETY RULES:

    AC SAFETY:
    ├── Treat all AC as lethal (120V+)
    ├── Use GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) in wet areas
    ├── Never work on live AC circuits
    ├── Use one hand when troubleshooting (prevents heart path)
    └── Verify power is OFF with a tester
    
    DC SAFETY:
    ├── Low voltage DC (<48V) is generally safe
    ├── High voltage DC (>100V) is as dangerous as AC
    ├── Car batteries (12V) are safe from shock but can cause sparks
    ├── EV batteries (400V-800V) are EXTREMELY DANGEROUS
    └── Capacitors can hold lethal charge even when powered off
```

## Measuring AC vs DC

```
MULTIMETER MEASUREMENT

DC MEASUREMENT:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    ├── Set dial to V (with straight line and dots) ───
    ├── Connect red to positive, black to negative
    ├── Meter shows average voltage (same as RMS for DC)
    └── Polarity matters (reverse shows negative)


AC MEASUREMENT:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
    ├── Set dial to V (with wavy line) ~
    ├── Polarity doesn't matter (AC alternates anyway)
    ├── Meter shows RMS voltage (for sine waves)
    └── For non-sine waves, need "True RMS" meter


OSCILLOSCOPE VIEW:

    DC on scope:
    ┼───────────── horizontal line at voltage level
    
    AC on scope:
    ╱╲    ╱╲    sine wave crossing center line
    
    DC with AC ripple:
    ┼╱╲╱╲─ horizontal line with small sine wave on top


TRUE RMS METERS:

    Regular multimeter: Assumes sine wave (will be wrong for square/triangle)
    True RMS meter: Correct for any waveform
    
    When you need True RMS:
    ├── Measuring AC from inverters (modified sine)
    ├── Measuring rectified AC
    ├── Measuring motor drive outputs (VFDs)
    └── Measuring audio signals
```

## Summary

1. **DC (Direct Current)** flows in one direction with constant polarity

2. **AC (Alternating Current)** reverses direction periodically (50 or 60 times/second)

3. **DC sources:** Batteries, solar cells, rectified AC, DC generators

4. **AC sources:** Power grid, alternators, inverters, oscillators

5. **RMS (Root Mean Square)** allows fair comparison: 120V AC RMS = 120V DC for heating

6. **AC won the War of Currents** because transformers enable efficient long-distance transmission

7. **DC never died** because electronics, batteries, and solar all need DC

8. **AC to DC conversion** uses rectifiers (diodes) and filters (capacitors)

9. **DC to AC conversion** uses inverters (modified or pure sine wave)

10. **AC is more dangerous** than DC at the same voltage (causes fibrillation)

11. **Low voltage DC (<48V)** is generally safe; high voltage DC is as dangerous as AC

12. **Modern systems use both:** AC for distribution, DC for electronics and storage
