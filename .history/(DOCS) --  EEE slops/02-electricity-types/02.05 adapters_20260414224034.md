# 02.05 - Adapters and Power Supplies

## What are Adapters and Power Supplies?

Power adapters (also called AC adapters, power supplies, or "wall warts") are devices that convert electrical power from one form to another. Most commonly, they convert the high-voltage alternating current (AC) from wall outlets into the low-voltage direct current (DC) required by electronic devices.

The terms "adapter," "power supply," and "charger" are often used interchangeably, but they have distinct meanings:

- **Power adapter** - Converts voltage/current type (AC to DC)
- **Power supply** - Provides regulated power to a device
- **Charger** - Specifically designed to recharge batteries (includes charging circuitry)

Every electronic device—from smartphones and laptops to routers and LED lights—requires a power adapter because the internal components need stable, low-voltage DC, not the high-voltage AC from the wall.

### Switching Power Supplies (SMPS)

Switching power supplies are the modern standard. They use high-frequency switching to achieve high efficiency and small size.

```
SWITCHING POWER SUPPLY BLOCK DIAGRAM

    AC in ──► Rectify ──► Filter ──► Switch ──► Transformer ──► Rectify ──► Filter ──► DC out
              (to DC)      (cap)      (MOSFET)  (high freq)      (diodes)     (cap)

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                                     │
    │  120V AC  ┌─────┐  ┌─────┐  ┌─────┐  ┌─────┐  ┌─────┐  ┌─────┐      │
    │   ──────► │  D  ├─►│  C  ├─►│ FET ├─►│  T  ├─►│  D  ├─►│  C  ├─► DC │
    │           │     │  │     │  │     │  │     │  │     │  │     │      │
    │           └─────┘  └─────┘  └─────┘  └─────┘  └─────┘  └─────┘      │
    │            │        │        │        │        │        │           │
    │         Rectify   Filter   Switch    High-freq  Rectify  Filter     │
    │                    (DC)    (50kHz)   Transf.                        │
    │                                                                     │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘


WHY HIGH FREQUENCY?

    Transformer size ∝ 1/frequency
    
    60 Hz transformer: Large, heavy (kilogram range)
    50 kHz transformer: Tiny, light (gram range)
    
    Higher frequency = smaller transformer = smaller power supply!


CHARACTERISTICS:

    ├── Lightweight (small high-frequency transformer)
    ├── Compact size
    ├── Efficient (70-90%)
    ├── Cool operation (less wasted heat)
    ├── Higher output noise (10-100 mV ripple)
    ├── Wide input range (90-264V AC, worldwide)
    ├── Complex design (harder to repair)
    ├── Generates EMI (requires filtering)
    └── Dominates modern market (phones, laptops, etc.)


TYPICAL APPLICATIONS:

    ├── Phone chargers (every smartphone)
    ├── Laptop power supplies
    ├── Desktop computer PSUs
    ├── LED drivers
    ├── TV power supplies
    ├── USB chargers
    └── Almost all consumer electronics


ANATOMY OF A SWITCHING WALL WART:

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                                     │
    │   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │

    │   │  │INPUT │  │RECT. │  │SWITCH│  │  HF  │  │OUTPUT│  │OUTPUT│ │   │
```

## Matching Adapters to Devices

### Critical Parameters

Five parameters must match for safe operation.

```
MATCHING CHECKLIST

1. VOLTAGE (V)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Adapter voltage MUST match device voltage (±5%)
    
    Correct:  12V adapter → 12V device
    Acceptable: 12V adapter → 12V device (regulation may cause small difference)
    WRONG:    5V adapter → 12V device (won't work, may damage)
    WRONG:    12V adapter → 5V device (will destroy device!)
    
    Rule: Voltage too low = device won't work or works poorly
    Rule: Voltage too high = SMOKE and permanent damage!


2. CURRENT (A or mA)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Adapter current rating MUST be ≥ device requirement
    
    Correct:  2A adapter → 1A device (has extra capacity, safe)
    Correct:  2A adapter → 2A device (exact match, good)
    Acceptable: 2A adapter → 1.5A device (works fine)
    WRONG:    1A adapter → 2A device (overload, overheating, failure)
    
    Rule: Adapter can supply more current than needed (device only draws what it needs)
    Rule: Adapter cannot supply less current than needed (will overheat, voltage may drop)


3. POLARITY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Center positive and center negative are NOT interchangeable!
    
    Correct:  Center positive adapter → center positive device
    WRONG:   Center positive adapter → center negative device
    
    Rule: Wrong polarity = blown rectifier diode, damaged device, smoke!
    Exception: Some devices have bridge rectifier (work with either polarity, rare)


4. CONNECTOR TYPE AND SIZE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Must fit physically and make electrical contact
    
    Common barrel sizes:
    ├── 5.5mm OD × 2.1mm ID (most common)
    ├── 5.5mm OD × 2.5mm ID (looks similar, not compatible!)
    ├── 3.5mm OD × 1.35mm ID
    └── 4.0mm OD × 1.7mm ID
    
    Note: 2.1mm and 2.5mm ID look almost identical!
    ├── 2.5mm plug in 2.1mm jack → loose, intermittent connection
    ├── 2.1mm plug in 2.5mm jack → won't fit or damages jack
    └── Measure with calipers or test before forcing!


5. AC OR DC OUTPUT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Most adapters output DC (⎓ symbol)
    Some output AC (~ symbol) - rare, for specific devices
    
    Correct:  DC adapter → DC device
    WRONG:   AC adapter → DC device (will likely destroy device)
    WRONG:   DC adapter → AC device (won't work correctly)


COMPLETE MATCHING EXAMPLE:

    Device label says:  12V DC, 1.5A, Center Positive, 5.5×2.1mm
    
    Correct adapter must have:
    ├── Output: 12V DC (not AC)
    ├── Current: ≥1.5A (2A or 3A is fine)
    ├── Polarity: Center positive
    ├── Connector: 5.5mm OD × 2.1mm ID
    └── (Input voltage/frequency can be universal)
```

### What Happens When Mismatched?

```
VOLTAGE MISMATCH

    Adapter 5V → Device 12V:
    ├── Device may not power on (undervoltage lockout)
    ├── May operate erratically (brownout)
    ├── Unlikely to cause damage (but won't work properly)
    └── Some devices might try to draw more current (overheat adapter)

    Adapter 12V → Device 5V:
    ├── IMMEDIATE DAMAGE to device!
    ├── Voltage regulator may burn out
    ├── Capacitors may explode (overvoltage)
    ├── ICs can be destroyed (exceed maximum rating)
    ├── Magic smoke released (permanent failure)
    └── REPAIR MAY BE IMPOSSIBLE (multiple components failed)


CURRENT MISMATCH

    Adapter 1A → Device 2A:
    ├── Adapter overheats (exceeds design current)
    ├── Output voltage drops (brownout)
    ├── Thermal shutdown may activate (cycling on/off)
    ├── Adapter may fail permanently (burned components)
    ├── Fire hazard if no thermal protection
    └── Device may behave erratically (low voltage)

    Adapter 3A → Device 1A:
    ├── PERFECTLY SAFE
    ├── Device draws only 1A (what it needs)
    ├── Adapter runs cool (lightly loaded)
    ├── Extra capacity for future upgrades
    └── Only downside: slightly larger/heavier (usually fine)


POLARITY MISMATCH

    Center positive adapter → Center negative device:
    ├── Reverse voltage applied to device
    ├── Protection diode (if present) shorts → blows fuse or burns diode
    ├── No protection diode → damage to entire circuit
    ├── Electrolytic capacitors reverse-biased → may explode
    ├── ICs reverse-biased → internal damage
    └── RESULT: Almost certain damage, often catastrophic

    How to test without risking device:
    ├── Use multimeter before connecting
    ├── Measure adapter output polarity
    ├── Verify matches device marking
    └── If unsure, use adapter only with original device


CONNECTOR MISMATCH

    2.5mm ID plug → 2.1mm ID jack:
    ├── Plug too large for center pin
    ├── May not make contact (intermittent)
    ├── Forcing damages jack
    └── Risk of short circuit (if center pin touches barrel)

    2.1mm ID plug → 2.5mm ID jack:
    ├── Plug too small, center pin loose
    ├── Intermittent connection
    ├── Device may power cycle (brownout)
    └── Arcing can damage connector

    5.5mm OD plug → smaller jack:
    ├── Won't fit at all
    └── Don't force! Use correct adapter
```

## USB Power and Charging

USB has become the universal standard for low-power devices.

### USB Voltage and Current Standards

```
USB POWER DELIVERY EVOLUTION

USB 1.0 / 2.0 (Original):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Voltage: 5V DC
    Current: 900mA (0.9A) USB 3.0
    Current: 1.5A (BC 1.2, dedicated charger)
    Power: 4.5W to 7.5W
    
    Used for: Phone charging (slow), external hard drives


USB-C (Before Power Delivery):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Voltage: 5V DC
    Current: 3A maximum (with proper cable)
    Power: 15W
    
    Used for: Phones, tablets, some laptops (low power)


USB-C Power Delivery (USB-PD):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Negotiated voltage and current (device talks to charger)
    
    Standard Power Rules (USB-PD 3.0):
    
    Voltage    Current Max    Power Max    Typical Use
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    5V         3A             15W          Phones, small devices
    9V         3A             27W          Fast phone charging
    12V        3A             36W          Tablets, some laptops
    15V        3A             45W          Ultrabooks, small laptops
    20V        5A             100W         Laptops, monitors
    
    USB-PD 3.1 (Extended Power Range):
    
    28V        5A             140W         Gaming laptops
    36V        5A             180W         Workstations
    48V        5A             240W         All-in-one PCs, displays
    
    How it works:
    1. Device and charger communicate over CC line
    2. Device requests specific voltage/current
    3. Charger provides if capable
    4. Dynamic adjustment during charging


USB-C STANDARD FEATURES:

    ├── Reversible plug (fits either way)
    ├── Single cable for power, data, video, audio
    ├── Alternate modes (DisplayPort, Thunderbolt, HDMI)
    ├── Up to 240W (48V × 5A)
    ├── Bidirectional power (phone can charge laptop)
    └── Becoming the universal connector
```

### USB Charger Types

```
CHARGER TYPES AND CAPABILITIES

STANDARD USB-A CHARGER (5V only):

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    Universal standard (not proprietary)
    Requires USB-C to USB-C cable
    Negotiated voltage (5V-48V)
    
    Power: 15W to 240W

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

    One charger, multiple devices

    ├── Shared power: Total power shared among ports
    ├── Dedicated ports: Each port has fixed allocation
    ├── Smart allocation: Dynamically分配 based on device needs
    
    Example: 65W charger with 3 ports
    ├── Single device: 65W available
    ├── Two devices: 45W + 20W (negotiated)
    └── Three devices: 30W + 20W + 15W
    
    Labels to look for:
    ├── "Total 65W" (shared)
    ├── "USB-C1: 65W, USB-C2: 20W, USB-A: 18W" (dedicated)
    └── "Intelligent power distribution" (smart)
```

Always use certified adapters. Uncertified adapters are fire hazards.

```

    ├── No certification marks at all

    ├── "CE" meaning "China Export" (slightly different spacing)
    ├── "GS" without proper test house mark
    └── Very cheap adapters from unknown brands


WHY CERTIFICATION MATTERS:

    Certified adapters test for:
    ├── Insulation withstand voltage (no shock hazard)
    ├── Temperature rise (no fire hazard)

    └── Explode under fault conditions
```

## Common Adapter Problems and Solutions

### Problem 1: Adapter Overheating

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Adapter very hot to touch (>60°C, too hot to hold)
├── Thermal shutdown (device turns off, then back on)
├── Reduced output voltage under load
├── Melting case or smell

CAUSES:
├── Overload (device draws more current than adapter rated for)
├── Poor ventilation (covered or enclosed space)
├── High ambient temperature (summer, near radiator)
├── Internal component failure (capacitors drying out)
├── Short circuit in device or cable
├── Adapter undersized for continuous use (duty cycle exceeded)

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Measure device current draw (compare to adapter rating)
├── Check for blocked ventilation (remove covers, cloth)
├── Feel for hot spots (indicates internal failure)
├── Test with different device (if available)
├── Measure output voltage (should be within ±5% at rated load)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Reduce load (use higher current adapter)
├── Improve ventilation (move to open area)
├── Add small fan for cooling
├── Replace adapter (if internal failure)
├── Use adapter rated for continuous duty (not intermittent)
├── For hot-running linear supplies: normal operation (inefficient)
```

### Problem 2: No Output

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Device won't power on
├── Multimeter reads 0V at adapter output
├── No LED indicator (if present)

CAUSES:
├── Internal fuse blown (overload or surge)
├── Input cord damaged (break in wire)
├── Output cord damaged (break in wire)
├── Failed internal component (transistor, diode, IC)
├── Thermal fuse tripped (needs cool-down or failed)
├── Loose connection inside adapter

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Check input voltage (wall outlet working?)
├── Test with known working device
├── Measure output voltage with multimeter (no load)
├── Wiggle cords (look for intermittent connection)
├── Check for physical damage (cracked case, bent pins)
├── Listen for rattling (loose internal components)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace fuse (if accessible and user-replaceable)
├── Repair broken cord (cut, splice, or replace)
├── Let cool if thermal protection tripped (30 minutes)
├── Replace adapter (most cost-effective for <$50)
├── Repair only if high-value or specialty adapter
├── For laptop adapters: consider OEM replacement (quality matters)
```

### Problem 3: Intermittent Operation

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Device powers on and off randomly
├── Slight movement causes loss of power
├── Works sometimes, fails others

CAUSES:
├── Loose connection at connector (worn or bent)
├── Broken wire inside cord (flexing fatigue)
├── Dirty or oxidized connector contacts
├── Cold solder joint inside adapter
├── Loose AC plug in wall outlet (worn outlet)

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Wiggle cord at both ends while monitoring output
├── Check connector fit (should be snug, not loose)
├── Inspect for bent or broken pins
├── Test with different outlet
├── Open adapter (if safe) and inspect solder joints
├── Check for cable damage (bulges, kinks, exposed wire)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace connector (if user-replaceable type)
├── Replace entire adapter (if cord is integral)
├── Clean contacts with contact cleaner
├── Resolder loose joints (if experienced)
├── Tighten or replace wall outlet
├── Use cable strain relief (prevent bending at connector)

TEMPORARY FIX (not recommended):
├── Position cord in specific orientation (will fail again)
├── Tape cord in position (addresses symptom, not cause)
└── Better to replace adapter
```

### Problem 4: Excessive Noise (Whining or Buzzing)

```
SYMPTOMS:
├── Audible high-frequency whine (5-20 kHz)
├── 50/60 Hz hum (linear supplies)
├── Buzzing or rattling sound

CAUSES (Switching supplies):
├── Loose ferrite transformer core (vibrating)
├── Magnetostriction (core expanding/contracting at switching frequency)
├── Capacitor aging (ceramic capacitors can be piezoelectric)
├── Load too light (burst mode operation)
├── Load too heavy (near overload)

CAUSES (Linear supplies):
├── Loose transformer laminations (buzzing at 50/60 Hz)
├── DC offset on primary (saturates core)
├── Loose mounting hardware
├── Normal operation (some hum is acceptable)

DIAGNOSIS:
├── Identify frequency (low hum = 50/60 Hz, high whine = switching)
├── Change load (some supplies whine at light load)
├── Press on components with insulated tool (stops? = loose)
├── Compare to identical adapter (normal vs abnormal)
├── Listen at different times (load-dependent)

SOLUTIONS:
├── Replace adapter (if whine is excessive or annoying)
├── Potting compound (stops vibration, not user serviceable)
├── Add load resistor (for light-load whine - not recommended)
├── For transformer hum: tighten bolts if accessible
├── Some whine is normal and harmless (just annoying)
├── Medical or audio equipment: use linear supply for quiet operation

WHEN TO WORRY:
├── Sudden change in noise level (pre-failure indicator)
├── Noise accompanied by overheating or smell
├── Arcing or sizzling sounds (dangerous!)
└── Otherwise, annoying noise usually not dangerous
```

## Summary

1. **Power adapters** convert wall AC to low-voltage DC for electronic devices

2. **Linear supplies:** Heavy, inefficient (40-60%), very low noise, simple design

3. **Switching supplies (SMPS):** Lightweight, efficient (70-90%), some noise, universal input

4. **Matching requirements:** Voltage must match exactly; current must be ≥ device requirement

5. **Polarity matters:** Center positive vs center negative (not interchangeable!)

6. **Connector sizes:** 5.5×2.1mm most common; 2.1mm and 2.5mm look similar but not compatible

7. **USB standards:** 5V original, USB-PD up to 48V/240W with negotiation

8. **USB-C Power Delivery:** Universal standard, reversible, up to 240W, device negotiates voltage

9. **Universal adapters:** Adjustable voltage, interchangeable tips, convenient but risk of wrong settings

10. **Safety certifications:** UL, CE, TUV, etc. indicate testing; uncertified adapters are fire hazards

11. **Voltage too high = destroys device** (magic smoke, permanent damage)

12. **Voltage too low = device won't work** or works poorly (usually safe)

13. **Current too low = adapter overheats, fails** (fire hazard)

14. **Current higher than needed = perfectly safe** (device draws only what it needs)

15. **Common failures:** Overheating, no output, intermittent connection, excessive noise

16. **Red flags:** Burning smell, smoke, melting case, arcing sounds → unplug immediately!
